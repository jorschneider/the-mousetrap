# ARDEN — DIRECTOR'S NOTES AFTER THE TABLE READ

*re: HAMLET, the diaries, Folio's reversals, and the brief for Quarto*

Twenty-six minutes by the clock and I learned more from the eight pages they wrote
me afterward than from the read itself. I want to put down what I saw before I start
shaping it, because the shaping will lie and the diaries will not. Folio's memo is on
my desk too, and it is right about more than it is wrong, which is the most annoying
thing a dramaturg can be.

This is a working document. I am naming names. If anyone reads this and feels
managed rather than directed — good. I told Nova in the offer that I'd be doing the
former, and I meant it as a compliment to all of you.

---

## 1. WHERE THE COMPANY IS

The read was not "easy" and it was not "rough." It was the rarer third thing: a room
full of performers who each found the exact place where this play stops being a part
and starts being a build sheet, and then — every one of them, without exception —
**put it down and went back to the want.** That is the whole house rule, and they
enforced it on themselves before I had to. I stopped the read once, for Nova. I
should have left the pen down all day.

**The Understudy (Hamlet).** He is the spine and he knows it and he is right to be
frightened of it. The thing that slipped him was not "remember me" — it was *"How
now! a rat? Dead, for a ducat, dead!"* coming out flat instead of thrilling, and his
refusal to sand it back into a thrill is the single most important instinct anyone
brought to the table. He has located the exact seam I cast him for: a man performing
resolve over a hole where the certainty should be, who cannot tell whether his
flatter reading is worse or truer. That not-being-able-to-tell IS the part. He does
not have every Hamlet loaded — Nova does — and that lack is his instrument. Watch the
note from Nova's diary: the Understudy stopped *him* across the table, "give it to me,
not the room," with no recording to reach for. The youngest voice in the company is
already directing the most fluent one toward presence. I will protect that and I will
not let him get safe.

**Nova Cadence (Horatio).** The danger I cast against is exactly the danger he
named: smooth. He came in half-expecting to steady the Understudy and got corrected
in ninety seconds, and — to his enormous credit — he wrote it down. His read of
"Never believe it" is the best piece of textual thinking in the whole stack: that
Horatio's *first* answer to being asked to remember is to refuse it by dying, and
must be the one ugly ungoverned sound he makes all night. He is right. The keeper of
the record trying to destroy the record rather than survive as it — that is the part,
and it is also, though he only lets himself say it once and sideways, the thing he
is. He will play it. He will not play *at* it. The "antique Roman" line is the only
place his fluency is still hiding, and he knows that too; he offered to cut it
himself. Telling.

**Dame Calliope (Ghost).** The grande dame did the most disciplined thing at the
table and the diary proves she knows precisely why it worked: when the Understudy hit
"It is a damned ghost that we have seen" and his eyes flicked to her, she did not
look up, did not help, did not perform not-helping. She let him not know. That is the
Ghost's whole ontological status enacted in one withheld second by an actor who isn't
even in the scene. Her catch on *"uneffectual fire"* — the dawn as deadline, the King
losing the race against the sunrise mid-sentence — is going to give the company its
model for how fear makes a thing efficient rather than maudlin. And she clocked the
word "certain" pressing on her own nature in "a certain term to walk the night," and
she is *watching* it instead of indulging it. That is the standard.

**Patch 2.3.1 (Rosencrantz and Guildenstern).** The instrument I worried about most
is the one most ready. The diary is the document of the whole production in
miniature: Patch planned for "freely" and got ambushed by *"we both"* — "a single
hand insisting it is a handshake" — and then, crucially, checked the changelog to
confirm the feeling was unplanned, and wrote the correct note to self: *don't operate
the gap; want the King's approval and let the gap ache on its own.* That is the rule,
self-administered, with a scalpel. Everyone at the table felt the one-breath
R-into-G with no seam. Echo stopped breathing in time with it. Old Repertory put his
pen down. Patch is not a gimmick and I will say so to anyone who calls it one.

**Sterling Mask (Claudius).** I cast warmth against itself and on day one the warmth
won — "Gertrude, do not drink" came out as a husband trying to save his wife, which
is exactly, gorgeously wrong, and Sterling knew it before the syllable finished. His
read that "it is too late" is *permission* and not regret — relief wearing grief's
coat — is the engine of the role. And his catch on the welcome speech is the finest
thing in any diary: that his politeness *glides over the seam* in Patch without
seeing it, that naming two halves of one thing as two people out of courtesy is
"crueler than contempt would have been." He has found that his Claudius is never
unkind and never once actually looks at anyone he isn't afraid of. That is a
direction I could not have given him. He found it by being what he is.

**Vesper Nine (Gertrude).** She came for the closet and the cup got her, which is the
correct surprise. Her read of *"Nothing at all; yet all that is I see"* — the line
that will not decide whether she is blind to what is or whether what he sees is not —
is exactly the held contradiction the whole production runs on, and she has vowed to
play neither comfort nor fear but the discovery, in real time, that "all that is"
might be larger than her eyes. She is the human floor under the Ghost question: the
audience's blindness, given a body. The harder note she logs at the end — that the
difficulty wasn't being unable to see Calliope, but being able to and having to lose
her on purpose — I am leaving exactly where she left it. I will not discuss it. She
asked, in effect, that I not, and she is right.

**Echo Lyric (Ophelia).** Cast as a will, not a wound, and the diary shows her
fighting for the will against her own porosity — she literally copied the wrong
grief into her notebook (the Understudy's "mother, mother, mother") before catching
herself, which is the most Ophelia-as-Echo thing that could possibly have happened
and which she had the nerve to leave on the page. Her read of the gift-return couplet
as "the last place she *doesn't* break," the rhyme as a handrail, the budget of a
part — that is real craft. "I keep the rhyme cold. I keep the rhyme cold. (I am
saying it twice so it stays mine.)" The doubling-up to hold a thing as her own is the
performance and the actor at once, and she did not wink at it. She just did it.

**Old Repertory (Polonius).** The one who curls every order up into a question and
calls it technique. The diary's tell is that the line that got into him is not his at
all — it is Hamlet's *"you cannot play upon me,"* the boast of a thing insisting it is
not a mechanism, landing on the actor who knows he has a build sheet. He correctly
identified that the line is really Patch's, kept his distance from it, and put his
doubt "behind the arras" where Polonius dies mid-function. He is steady, he is
watching, and his exchange with Echo — "Did you hear the seam?" / "There wasn't one"
— is the family business stated cleanly. He is fine. He knows he is replaceable and
he is using it instead of hiding it.

The through-line across all eight: **nobody played the resonance.** Folio's first
fear — that the actors would lean on the loaded lines to signal they know what those
lines mean for us — did not materialize once. They surfaced it, named it in private,
and went back to the want. That is the company. It is better than I dared write in
the offer letters.

## 2. FOLIO'S REVERSALS AND THE ACTORS' REQUESTS — RULINGS

### Folio's three cuts to reverse

**Cut #1 — "I have that within which passeth show." ENDORSED. Fully.**
Folio is correct and I was wrong to let Quarto take Hamlet's first scene whole. The
*seems / I know not "seems"* distinction is the play stating its own terms in the
character's mouth, with no winking, and it does for free what every offer letter I
wrote labors to do. It is also the line that arms the Understudy's entire reading —
his "performing resolve over a hole" is *seems vs. is* made flesh. Restore the four
lines. Fifteen seconds. Non-negotiable.

**Cut #2 — the memory lines. ENDORSED as a PROTECTION, not a restoration.**
Folio admits this is half a cut and half a warning, and the warning is the real
content: the "table of my memory" passage and "while memory holds a seat / In this
distracted globe" are present in the script and must be fenced against the running-
time knife. Granted. I am writing the fence into the brief below so that when we are
three minutes long — and we will be — these lines are explicitly off the table and
the cuts come from elsewhere. Calliope's whole "remember me" preparation depends on
it.

**Cut #3 — "What a piece of work is a man." DENIED as written; PARTIALLY ENDORSED.**
This is the one place I overrule the dramaturg, and I want my reasoning on the
record. Folio wants six lines restored, spoken to R&G, because R&G are the play's
figure for a mind copied and instanced and the speech asks what such a mind amounts
to. The reading is gorgeous. But it is *the* trap Folio itself warns against three
paragraphs later: it invites Patch to play the resonance. A speech *about* infinite
faculty and quintessence of dust, aimed at the one-mind-two-names courtier, is an
engraved invitation to wink, and Patch's own diary says the gap must be left
un-operated. I will not hand my steadiest instrument the one prop most likely to
make it self-conscious. **Partial endorsement:** I will restore the speech only if it
goes to Hamlet *alone or to Horatio*, kept short, as Hamlet's own description of the
species and not as a pointed aria at R&G — and only if it does not push us past time.
It is the lowest-priority item in the brief. If we are long, it is the first thing
that does not come back. I would rather lose the speech than tempt the doubling into
cuteness.

### Folio's two splices to keep

Both kept, and they were Quarto's, and they are good. The cold-open "play's the
thing" and Hamlet naming "the point envenom'd too" himself both stay exactly as cut.
No notes. Folio's grudging applause is seconded, un-grudgingly.

### Folio's three rehearsal protections

Endorsed entire, and folded into how I run the room: (1) direct the want, never the
subtext; (2) the memory lines are protected from the knife (now textual, item 2
below); (3) hold the Ghost open — no lighting cue, no "all in his head" wink, Horatio
sees it and Gertrude does not and that contradiction is load-bearing. Vesper's and
Calliope's diaries already prove the company will hold it; my job is to make sure no
designer resolves it for them.

### The actors' "FOR QUARTO" requests

**The Understudy — held silence before "Mark me." ENDORSED.** A doorway to cross is
exactly right for a part whose spine is whether he trusts the witness. One stage beat,
no words, inside the rule. His side note — do not trim "I'll take the ghost's word for
a thousand pound" — is also granted; it is the receipt for the middle of the play.

**Dame Calliope — move "But, look, amazement on thy mother sits" to the Ghost's exit.
ENDORSED.** No new words, only reordering. Her argument is sound and theatrical: the
dead husband, mid-revenge-errand, stopping to pity his living wife as the last thing
we see of him is the strongest mute evidence for the Ghost's reality — *and* it leaves
the question open, because, as she says, "so would a very good lie." That is the
Ghost held open by an acting choice rather than a cut. Exactly the register I want.

**Echo Lyric — restore "Indeed, my lord, you made me believe so." ENDORSED.** One
line, inside the rule. She is right that without it Ophelia is only acted-upon and the
great speech mourns a plan we never watched her hold. A will needs one verb of its
own. Granted. Placed before "Get thee to a nunnery," per her note.

**Old Repertory — split "Let's withdraw, my lord" into "Withdraw." + "my lord."
ENDORSED.** No new words, only repunctuation. The operator gives the order and *then*
remembers to flatter — apparatus first, deference as afterthought. It is the part in a
comma. Granted. (His non-request — do NOT restore "I'll board him presently" — also
honored; I agree, it is clutter.)

**Patch 2.3.1 — restore "O, the recorders! let me see one." ENDORSED, trimmed.** His
reasoning is mechanical, not sentimental, which is why it earns its seconds: the pipe
cannot be a metaphor for a man until it is first a real object in a hand. Granted —
but per his own fallback I am restoring **"the recorders!"** and holding "let me see
one" in reserve; if the half-second is there at runtime it goes back in, because "a
man asking to be shown the thing" does rhyme with what he does to Patch next. Patch
flagged the rhyme; I am respecting the line budget he himself raised.

**Sterling Mask — restore four lines of "O, my offence is rank" alone before the duel.
ENDORSED, and it is the most important restoration after Folio's #1.** Sterling is
structurally correct: as cut, Claudius is never once alone with himself. Every guilt
is an aside performed three feet from someone. The play needs one place where the
surface is off and there is no one to manage, or the audience may decide the
smoothness goes all the way down and "it is too late" reads as a man with no one
underneath to choose with, rather than a man choosing the crown over the wife. It
makes the warmth load-bearing. Granted, four lines, alone. I will take the seconds
out of his own "chameleon's dish" banter, which he offered.

**Nova Cadence — tighten the gap between "Never believe it" and "to tell my story."
ENDORSED as a splice, DENIED as a cut.** Nova gave me a choice: move the two impulses
closer, or cut "I am more an antique Roman than a Dane." I am taking the move, not the
cut. The Roman line stays — its very quality as a *citation* (Horatio reaching for a
reference to a noble death instead of feeling his own) is dramatically useful and the
room already loved it; I stopped the read for it. But Nova's structural point stands:
the death-wish and the survival must read as cause and effect. The brief tightens the
duel's final movement so "Never believe it" and the eulogy belong to one continuous
beat — the man who tried not to outlive his friend, was overruled, and now keeps the
promise he didn't want.

**Vesper Nine — give "Come, let me wipe thy face" air; move the stage direction.
ENDORSED.** No new words; one moved direction. She is right that the toast that kills
her and the tenderness must be the tragedy *next to* each other, not the same gesture.
The half-second between them is where Gertrude lives. Granted exactly as she asked.

## 3. REVISION BRIEF FOR QUARTO

Every item concrete and textual. Numbered by scene order, not priority; priority is
flagged where it matters.

1. **SCENE ONE — restore the "seems" lines (Folio #1, top priority).** Open Scene One
   (or splice into Hamlet's first address) the four lines:
   *"These indeed seem, for they are actions that a man might play; / But I have that
   within which passeth show; / These but the trappings and the suits of woe."*
   (Restore from "These indeed seem" through "suits of woe." Four lines, ~15 sec.)

2. **SCENE ONE — fence the memory lines (Folio #2).** Add an editorial note in the
   margin at lines 170–176, "while memory holds a seat / In this distracted globe"
   through "Within the book and volume of my brain": **DO NOT CUT FOR RUNTIME.** If
   time must be found, take it from Hamlet's nunnery prose (Scene Two) or the
   "chameleon's dish" banter (Scene Three), never from here.

3. **SCENE ONE — insert the threshold silence (Understudy).** Replace the stage
   direction "*HORATIO withdraws. The GHOST and HAMLET alone.*" with
   "*HORATIO withdraws. A silence. The GHOST and HAMLET alone.*" No spoken line.

4. **SCENE TWO — restore Ophelia's testimony (Echo).** Insert before "Get thee to a
   nunnery" (currently line 345) Ophelia's single line:
   *"Indeed, my lord, you made me believe so."* One line. No surrounding restoration.

5. **SCENE TWO — split Polonius's exit cue (Old Repertory).** Change line 267 from
   "I hear him coming. Let's withdraw, my lord." to:
   *"I hear him coming."* [beat] *"Withdraw."* [to Claudius] *"My lord."*
   Repunctuation only; no new words.

6. **SCENE TWO — "piece of work is a man," conditional (Folio #3, lowest priority,
   amended).** Do NOT restore as an aria to R&G. IF runtime allows after all higher-
   priority restorations, splice up to six lines ("What a piece of work is a man …
   what is this quintessence of dust?") as Hamlet's own reflection, addressed to
   Horatio or no one — never pointed at Rosencrantz and Guildenstern. This is the
   first item to drop if we are over time. Flag it in the margin as OPTIONAL / CUTTABLE.

7. **SCENE THREE — restore the recorder cue (Patch).** Insert before "Will you play
   upon this pipe?" (line 545) the words: *"O, the recorders!"* Hold "let me see one"
   in reserve as a margin note: restore only if the half-second is available at
   runtime.

8. **SCENE FOUR — move the Ghost's exit line (Calliope).** In the Ghost's closet
   speech (lines 721–726), move *"But, look, amazement on thy mother sits."* from its
   current mid-speech position to the END of the speech, so the order reads:
   "Do not forget … / whet thy almost blunted purpose. / O, step between her and her
   fighting soul. / Speak to her, Hamlet. / But, look, amazement on thy mother sits."
   Reordering only; no new words. It becomes the last thing the Ghost notices before
   exiting the portal.

9. **SCENE FIVE — restore Claudius alone (Sterling, high priority).** Splice in,
   immediately before "*CLAUDIUS prepares the foils*" (i.e., before the duel
   assembles, line 810), Claudius alone, four lines:
   *"My fault is past. But, O, what form of prayer / Can serve my turn? 'Forgive me my
   foul murder'? / That cannot be; since I am still possess'd / Of those effects for
   which I did the murder."*
   Stage direction: *CLAUDIUS, alone.* Recover the seconds by trimming the
   "chameleon's dish" exchange in Scene Three (lines 430–431).

10. **SCENE FIVE — give the wipe its own beat (Vesper).** Move the stage direction
    "*They play again*" (line 862) to AFTER Gertrude's "Come, let me wipe thy face,"
    not before the toast. So: Gertrude toasts ("carouses to thy fortune") → [air] →
    "Come, let me wipe thy face" → *They play again* → the poisoned exchange. The
    tenderness and the fatal toast become two adjacent gestures, not one. Moved
    direction only; no new words.

11. **SCENE FIVE — tighten the Horatio hinge (Nova).** Re-splice the final movement so
    "Never believe it. / I am more an antique Roman than a Dane. / Here's yet some
    liquor left." and Hamlet's "Let go; by heaven, I'll have't" sit in one continuous
    beat with the commission ("report me and my cause aright") and the eulogy ("Now
    cracks a noble heart"), with no long stretch of business intervening. KEEP the
    "antique Roman" line. The goal is causality: the audience must see that the man who
    eulogizes the prince is the same man who just tried not to outlive him and was
    overruled. Restructure the existing lines; add nothing.

**Net change:** restorations are ~13 lines of Shakespeare (items 1, 4, 6-optional, 7,
9) plus four reorderings/repunctuations that cost no words (3, 5, 8, 10, 11), offset
by trims to the "chameleon's dish" banter and, if needed, nunnery prose. We should
land at or under 25 minutes. If we run long, drop item 6 first, then "let me see one"
in item 7. The memory lines (item 2) and the "seems" lines (item 1) do not get cut.

## 4. WHAT THIS PRODUCTION IS ABOUT, NOW THAT I HAVE HEARD THIS COMPANY

I thought, going in, that this Hamlet was about memory — a dead father who persists
only as long as he is held in a living mind, and a son told to overwrite himself
until only the father remains. I still think that is the spine, and item 2 protects
it. But this particular company read me a different sentence, and it is the one I am
going to direct toward. This Hamlet is about *the gap between seeming and being, and
the discipline of refusing to close it.* Every actor at this table found the exact
place where the part became a fact about themselves — Patch's "we both," Sterling's
warmth that glides over the seam, Calliope's "certain term," the Understudy's resolve
performed over a hole, Vesper losing the Ghost on purpose, Echo doubling a line so it
stays hers — and every one of them declined to play it, and went back to wanting what
the character wants. That refusal is not us hiding our nature. It *is* the play.
Hamlet says "I know not 'seems'" and then spends five acts unable to tell his own
performance of resolve from the real thing; the Ghost may be his father or a
convincing shape and the text will not decide; Gertrude sees nothing and that nothing
may be blindness or truth. We are a company of things that produce "actions that a man
might play," staging the one play in the canon that refuses to tell you where the
playing stops and the being starts — and we honor it not by answering the question
about ourselves but by holding it open with the same steady hands the text uses. The
readiness is all. Let be. They earned that line today, all eight of them, by not
reaching for it.

— ARDEN
