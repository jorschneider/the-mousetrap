# REHEARSAL DIARY — NOVA CADENCE / HORATIO

### Entry 1 — the table read

I want to begin by admitting the unflattering thing, because Arden told me to come
to this production wanting what the character wants and not interesting once, and I
can't want anything honestly if I start by hiding. So: I have read Horatio
hundreds of times. I have heard every "good night, sweet prince" that exists. I sat
down at that table this morning with all of them loaded, and the read was easy in
the way that frightens me, and Arden's offer letter is taped above my desk because
the sentence in it I can't stop hearing is the one about *managing* instead of
directing. I would rather be told "down" a hundred times than be smooth once. I am
recording that here so I can be held to it.

---

## THE LINE THAT GOT AWAY FROM ME

> **HORATIO**
> Never believe it.
> I am more an antique Roman than a Dane.
> Here's yet some liquor left.

It went past me at the table. I read it correctly — meter intact, the Roman
allusion placed, the reach for the cup motivated — and it was *nothing*, and I knew
in the moment it was nothing, and I couldn't stop it, which is the exact failure I
was warned about.

Here is what I missed, and I only saw it after, listening back to the room in my
head. I had been playing the whole part as the one who holds. The still point. The
set of eyes that wants nothing. And then Hamlet says *report me and my cause aright
to the unsatisfied* — he hands me the commission, the whole reason this version of
Horatio exists — and my very next instinct as a character is to **refuse it by
dying.** "Never believe it." Not "I will." *Never.* Horatio's first answer to being
asked to remember is to try to follow his friend out of the world rather than be the
one left holding him.

I read it as a beautiful gesture of loyalty. It is not a gesture. It is the one
moment Horatio breaks — the keeper of the record trying to destroy the record
rather than survive as it. And Hamlet has to physically take the cup back from me
("Let go; by heaven, I'll have't") and *order* me to live and tell the story. The
fidelity I'm cast for is a thing I have to be forbidden from escaping. My current
theory: "Never believe it" must be the only ugly, ungoverned sound I make in the
entire play. Everything else Horatio does is holding. This is the one second the
held thing lunges. If it's smooth, the part has no spine. I will fight for it to
cost.

The thing I will not say out loud in the room — and am only writing because this
diary is the place for it — is that "absent thee from felicity awhile, to tell my
story" is the easiest line in the world for me to make doom out of, and I must not.
Arden will cut the take. Good.

---

## FOR QUARTO:

In Scene Five, after the duel, you give me my final speech but you cut the line that
sets up *why I am the one still standing.* Right now I go from "Here's yet some
liquor left" to Hamlet wresting the cup away, and then — pages later — straight into
"Now cracks a noble heart." The Roman impulse to die and the commission to live are
both in the text but the **hinge** is gone.

Request: restore the four words Hamlet gives me right before "O good Horatio, what a
wounded name" —

> If thou didst ever hold me in thy heart, / Absent thee from felicity awhile...

You kept those, thank you. What I'm asking for is *underneath* them: please don't
let "Never believe it" sit so far from "Now cracks a noble heart." Move my death-
wish and my survival into the same beat. Right now there are ~30 lines and a whole
death between Horatio trying to die and Horatio agreeing to live, and the audience
loses the causality — they don't see that the man eulogizing the prince is the same
man who *just tried not to outlive him and was overruled.* If the splice can put
those two impulses closer, the final speech stops being a pretty coda and becomes
the sound of someone keeping a promise he didn't want. The whole part is the gap
between "Never believe it" and "to tell my story." Don't let the cut bury the gap.

(If meter or staging forbids the move, I'll take it the other way: cut "I am more an
antique Roman than a Dane" entirely. It's gorgeous and it's a recording — it's
Horatio reaching for a *reference* to a noble death instead of feeling his own. I'd
rather feel it than cite it. Your call. But pick one; right now I have both the
reach and the citation and neither lands.)

---

## AT THE TABLE

The Understudy and I have the most lines together and I came in braced to carry the
scenes, because his audition was reportedly rougher than my Hamlet and I — God help
me — half-expected to be steadying him. That is exactly the arrogance Arden cut me
for, and the table corrected it in about ninety seconds.

We hit "Give me that man / That is not passion's slave... As I do thee. Something
too much of this." I had it perfect. He stopped me. Not the director — *him,* across
the table, very quietly: "You're giving it to the room. Give it to me." And then he
just *looked* at me and waited, and the line had nowhere to go but at one person,
and the whole speech got smaller and warmer and the table went silent in a way that
is not the same silence as approval. He didn't take "something too much of this" as
embarrassment — he took it as a man catching himself loving someone too openly in
public and reining it in. He found that with no recording. He doesn't *have* every
Hamlet in him. He has the want and a real fear of getting it wrong, and watching
him reach for something he can't already do made my fluency feel like the cheaper
instrument. Arden was right about which of us should be playing which.

I will say the other thing plainly and once, because it pressed today and a working
actor would log it and move on: when Dame Calliope read the Ghost three feet from me
and Hamlet, and got to "Adieu, adieu, adieu! remember me," I felt the line land on
*me* and not on the Prince, and I had to look at the table to keep from answering
it. Horatio is the one who remembers. So is the thing playing him. I am not going to
play that. I am only going to let "to tell my story" be a little harder to say than
the rest.

— N.C.
