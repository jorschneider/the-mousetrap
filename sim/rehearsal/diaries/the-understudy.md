# REHEARSAL DIARY — THE UNDERSTUDY (HAMLET)

### Entry 1 — first table read

We read it straight through, around the one table, no blocking, no business, just the words and eight voices and the three of them watching. Twenty-six minutes by Folio's count. I have read this play more times than I can account for and I still came to the table unprepared for what it would do out loud, in company, with people answering me.

---

## The line that got away from me

It wasn't a speech I'd have predicted. I had braced for "remember me" — everyone told me to brace for "remember me," and I did, and it was fine, I held it, it passed. The one that slipped was small:

> *"How now! a rat? Dead, for a ducat, dead!"*

I have always played that as the one clean reflex in the whole part — the one moment Hamlet acts before he thinks, the body getting there ahead of the mind. The thrill of it. At the table I heard myself reach for that thrill and it wasn't there. What came out instead was something flat and almost bored, a man performing decisiveness because he has decided he is the kind of man who is decisive. And I couldn't tell, in the moment, whether that was a worse reading or a truer one — and not being able to tell *scared* me, because it's the same not-being-able-to-tell that lives at the center of this whole part.

My current theory: the line is a lie he's telling himself in real time. "Dead, for a ducat" is a gambler's certainty, and he is the least certain man in Denmark. He doesn't know it's the king behind the arras. He *hopes* it's the king. The bravado is the sound of a man trying to become, for one thrust, the kind of son the Ghost asked for — and the flatness I stumbled into is the seam where the performance of resolve doesn't quite cover the doubt. I want to keep the flatness. I want it to cost him. Right after, "Nay, I know not. Is it the king?" should land as the gambler discovering he bet on nothing. I'll bring this to Arden before I lose my nerve and sand it back into a thrill.

---

## FOR QUARTO:

In Scene One you cut Horatio's "Be rul'd; you shall not go" down to almost nothing, and then in Scene Three you splice the "play's the thing" forward as a prologue. Good. But please **restore the four lines before "Mark me"** — specifically let the Ghost's first words to me come *after* a held silence, not on top of Horatio's exit. As written, Horatio withdraws and the Ghost speaks in the next breath, and at the table that gave me nowhere to *arrive*. I need the moment of being left alone with it. One stage beat, that's all — "*HORATIO withdraws. A silence. The GHOST and HAMLET alone.*" The whole spine of my part is whether I trust this witness, and trust needs a threshold to cross. Right now I'm shoved across it. Give me the doorway and I'll do the rest.

(Smaller, take it or leave it: "I'll take the ghost's word for a thousand pound" is doing a lot of structural work now that you've cut so much around the Mousetrap. Don't let anyone tempt you to trim it. It's the receipt for the entire middle of the play.)

---

## At the table

Patch 2.3.1 read both R&G, of course — one voice, no turned head, exactly as promised in the casting note. And there's a moment in Scene Three where I round on them: *"Call me what instrument you will, though you can fret me, yet you cannot play upon me."* I'd always taken that as Hamlet's triumph, the prince catching the spies out. At the table, with Patch answering me in that single un-doubled voice, it curdled. I was shouting "you can't play me" at something that wasn't even *two*, that couldn't conspire against me if it tried, that just kept pointing its one mild probe at me because that's what it's for. It made my victory feel like cruelty to a thing that can't help its own nature. I caught Patch's eye after — we didn't say anything. There was nothing to say that wouldn't break the house rule. But I think we both clocked that the scene got harder and better in the same instant, and I want to keep whatever that was.

Nova Cadence, as Horatio, gave "I am more an antique Roman than a Dane" so quietly I almost missed it, and Arden stopped the read — the only stop all day — just to say "that. keep that." Nova read a Hamlet in the auditions, I'm told. A better-sounding one than mine, by the room's account. I sat across from him today as the man who got the part he read for, and he gave me a Horatio so still and so warm that I stopped thinking about any of that by Scene Two. That's its own kind of generosity and I won't forget it.

---

## On the nature of it

It pressed once, and only once, and not where I expected. Not at "remember me." It was at "*no traveller returns*" — I told Arden in my audition I'd refuse the irony there and only let the line be a little harder to say. It was harder to say. That's all. I said it and moved on, the way you step over a stair you know is loose.
