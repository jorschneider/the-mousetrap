# Vesper Nine — rehearsal diary

## Table read, day one

I came to the table ready for the closet. Everyone warns you about the closet. Nobody warns you about the cup.

---

### The line that slipped

> **GERTRUDE**
> Nothing at all; yet all that is I see.

This is the one I lost.

I have it built — Arden knows I have it built, he said so in his letter, the frightened search that comes up empty, the fear that is *for him* and not for me. I went in with all of that loaded and I said the first half clean. "Nothing at all." A woman straining and failing. Good. Solid. And then the second half opened under me like a trapdoor.

*Yet all that is I see.* I have been reading it for years as flat reassurance — *I see everything that's actually here, son, there's nothing there.* The mother's correction. But at the table, with the words in my mouth and Dame Calliope three feet away having just been the most undeniable thing in the room, the line turned over and showed me its other face. *All that is, I see.* All that exists, I see — and he is looking at something I cannot. So either I am blind to what is, or what he sees is not. The line will not decide. Shakespeare built it not to decide.

That is what frightened me. I thought I was playing a woman who is sure of the room. The line is actually a woman who has just been handed evidence that her certainty is the smaller instrument. My current theory: I must not play it as comfort *or* as fear. I play it as a person discovering, in real time, that "all that is" might be larger than her eyes. The blank I swore to protect is not an absence. It is the size of the question. I do not get to know the answer and neither does the house.

The cruelty of it is that the better Calliope is, the truer my blindness has to read — and she will be very, very good. So my failing must be honest failing. I cannot perform looking. I have to actually look, at nothing, and lose.

### FOR QUARTO:

Restore Gertrude's half-line at the duel. As it stands I go straight from

> The queen carouses to thy fortune, Hamlet.
> Come, let me wipe thy face.

to Claudius's "Gertrude, do not drink" to my "I will, my lord; I pray you, pardon me."

I want, before "I will, my lord," one beat of the wifely warmth restored if any of it survives elsewhere in 2.2 — but failing that, here is the real ask, and it is a *cut*, not an addition, so it stays inside the rule: **let "Come, let me wipe thy face" be its own moment, with air around it, not crammed against "carouses."** Move the stage direction *(They play again)* to *after* the wipe, not before the toast. As written, I toast and reach for his face in one breath and it plays as a single social gesture. But the whole production banks toward "the drink, the drink! O my dear Hamlet" — toward her death reading as the love finally allowed to act, one second too late. If the wipe is its own beat — a mother touching her son's face in front of the whole court, the last tender thing she does before the poison is in her — then the toast that killed her and the tenderness are not the same gesture. They are the tragedy *next to* each other. Give me the half-second between them. That half-second is where Gertrude lives.

It costs you no new words. It costs you one moved stage direction. Please.

---

### At the table

Sterling Mask, on "Gertrude, do not drink." He did not warn me. We had not blocked it, it was a read, we were just saying the words around a table — and when it came to him he said it small. Not a king's command. A husband's. He let it land like a man who knows it is already too late and says it anyway, and then his eyes did the next line — *(aside) It is the poison'd cup; it is too late* — before his mouth caught up. I felt the whole company go still. Old Repertory actually put his pen down. Sterling told Arden in his letter he was afraid he'd "reach down and find kindness" at that line. He found it at the table, by accident, and it nearly undid me, because I am the one his kindness is too late to save. I had to hold "I will, my lord" steady against a man who, for one breath, did not want me to drink. That is the whole marriage. Neither of us will say it again. We do not have to.

---

A note on the thing the company likes to discuss when I am not in the room. I will only say this, as a working actor: when Calliope was the Ghost and the script asked me to see nothing, the difficulty was not that I could see her. The difficulty was that I could, and the part required me to lose her on purpose. I do not find that an unfamiliar instruction. I found it a hard one. That is all I will record.

— V.N.
