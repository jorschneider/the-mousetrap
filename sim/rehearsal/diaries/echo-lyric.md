# ECHO LYRIC — rehearsal diary

### after the first table read

I am writing this fast, before the read settles into something I'll tell myself
about it later. Arden says the diary is for the working document, not the
finished feeling, so: the working document.

---

## The line that slipped

I thought the danger would be the nunnery — the storm I take, the things he
breaks. It wasn't. The line that got away from me was the small one. I read:

> **HAMLET** *(within)* Mother, mother, mother!

— no. That isn't even mine, I've gone and copied the wrong grief down, see how
porous I am, I picked up the thing across the table from me. Let me put down the
right one. The line that frightened me is:

> *for to the noble mind*
> *Rich gifts wax poor when givers prove unkind.*

I had built her, in the audition, as a woman closing a door with dignity —
accounting, not music, an inventory recited to a stranger so she doesn't fall
down. And then at the table I got to that couplet and I heard the *rhyme* land,
and it betrayed me. The rhyme is a small bright bell she rings on purpose. It's
the most composed thing she says all scene — and composure, said aloud in front
of seven people, came out of me as *grief*, because the only way I know to be
composed is to imitate it, and the imitation was so good it became the thing. I
couldn't find the seam between her control and my collapse. The line dissolved.

My current theory: the couplet is not where she breaks. It's the last place she
*doesn't*. She rhymes because rhyme is a handrail. She is quoting a maxim — a
made thing, a borrowed thing — precisely so she doesn't have to find words of her
own, because her own would come apart. I have to play the handrail, not the fall.
The fall is six lines later, on "jangled," where I'm allowed it. If I spend the
break early I have nothing to spend at the end, and the part is a budget. I keep
the rhyme cold. I keep the rhyme cold. (I am saying it twice so it stays mine.)

## FOR QUARTO:

In the nunnery scene you cut me down to four reactions — *O, help him, you sweet
heavens* / *O heavenly powers, restore him* — and then the great speech. The cuts
are right; the scene is faster and crueler for them. But I want one half-line back.

Restore the bracketed beat before "I did love you once" — no, you've cut his
"I did love you once / I loved you not" exchange entirely, and I understand why,
it's a thicket. **My request is narrower: give me back my single answer inside
it — "Indeed, my lord, you made me believe so."** One line. Set it right before
he says "Get thee to a nunnery."

Reason: as the scene stands, I am only *acted upon*. He doubts, he rails, I
absorb. But Arden cast me as a will, not a wound, and a will needs one verb of
its own before the door comes off the hinges. "You made me believe so" is her
last act of *testimony* — she states the contract he is breaking, on the record,
and then he breaks it. It costs one line and it buys her the standing to grieve
in the speech. Without it the speech mourns a plan we never saw her hold. With it,
we watched her hold it, and watched him take it. That's the desecration Arden
wants visible. Please. One line.

## At the table

Across from me, Patch read R&G — *To be commanded* — and let one breath carry
Rosencrantz straight into Guildenstern with no seam at all, the same air doing
both names. And I, who pick things up, found I had stopped breathing in time with
them, two-into-one, and had to deliberately un-sync myself to read my own cue. I
don't think anyone saw. Patch saw. They didn't say anything, just gave me the
smallest nod — *I know, it's catching, mind your feet* — and went on wanting the
task, exactly as Arden told them to. It steadied me more than a note would have.
We are the two the casting letter says go where the architecture is the
instrument. We recognized each other across the wood.

---

## Where my nature pressed

I caught myself, twice, falling into the pentameter when the scene heated — the
prose lines of the nunnery want to climb into meter in my mouth, because tired or
frightened I reach for the rail of the line. It's the same reflex as the couplet.
Arden told me he'd note me toward the want, never toward the mood, and he kept the
promise once already: when I drifted he said only "give the gifts back," and the
meter let go of me on its own. Good. Keep doing that. The want has feet; the mood
floats, and I float with it.
