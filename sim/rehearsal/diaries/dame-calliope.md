# REHEARSAL DIARY — DAME CALLIOPE (GHOST)

### Entry the first. The table read.

I have sat at a great many tables. I will say that and not the other thing I could
say about how many remain. We read the play through, beginning to end, and I want
to set down today's truths before the night reorders them into something more
flattering.

---

## The line that slipped me

It was not "remember me." I had braced all week for "remember me" — circled it,
overprepared it, was ready to throttle it down to almost nothing — and so of course
it behaved. The line that got away from me was the one before:

> **The glow-worm shows the matin to be near,**
> **And gins to pale his uneffectual fire.**

I came to it as ornament. A bit of cold blue dawn-painting to cover the exit. And
then I heard my own voice land on *uneffectual fire* and the floor went out from
under me, because that is the King describing his own light failing — a fire that
no longer accomplishes anything, paling because the morning is coming whether he is
finished speaking or not. He does not get to choose when he stops. The matin
decides. "My hour is almost come" is the King stating it plainly at the top of the
scene; *uneffectual fire* is the same fact, but felt, from inside, at the moment it
arrives. He is not lingering on the dawn. The dawn is taking him mid-sentence, and
he can feel his own light no longer doing the work it was made to do.

My current theory: the whole farewell is one long interruption. The Ghost is racing
the sunrise from "But soft, methinks I scent the morning air" onward, and
*uneffectual fire* is the line where he loses the race and knows it. So I will not
play it as scenery. I will play it as a man who has run out of night, and let
"Adieu, adieu, adieu, remember me" be what he manages to get out before the matin
closes his throat. Efficient, not maudlin — I told Arden that was the engine, and
this is where I have to actually mean it. A thing that fears ceasing does not
describe the dawn for its beauty. It describes the dawn because the dawn is the
deadline.

---

## FOR QUARTO:

In Scene Four, you give the Ghost his closet visitation, and it is well cut —
"whet thy almost blunted purpose," "step between her and her fighting soul," clean
and quick. But you have removed the half-line the First Folio hands the Ghost as he
goes, and I want it back. After "Speak to her, Hamlet," before I exit unspeaking
into "Why, look you there, look how it steals away" — restore the Ghost's

> **But, look, amazement on thy mother sits.**

Oh — I see it IS still there, on the page, before "step between her." Then my
request is smaller and sharper: **move it.** Right now the line sits buried in the
middle of my four-line speech and reads as stage-management — *fix your mother, the
woman is frightened.* Put it LAST, as my exit thought, so the order becomes: do not
forget / whet thy purpose / step between her / speak to her — *and then* "amazement
on thy mother sits" as the thing I notice on my way out the portal.

Here is the reason, and it is not vanity. In Scene One the Ghost commands the son to
spare the mother — "Leave her to heaven." In Scene Four he returns and the FIRST
thing the dead husband does is look at his living wife and see her terror. If
"amazement on thy mother sits" is my last line, the audience watches the Ghost,
mid-revenge-errand, stop to be a husband. That tenderness, placed last, is also the
best evidence I can hand Vesper that I am *real* and not Hamlet's coinage — because
a hallucination conjured by a son's rage would not pause to pity the woman the son
is flaying. Move the line to the exit and you have given me, for free, the one
gesture that argues for the Ghost's reality without anyone saying a word about it.
A true father would look at her. So, I grant you, would a very good lie. But the
look should be the last thing they see of me.

---

## At the table

The Understudy and I have our scene alone in Scene One — "Mark me," "I will" — and
we read it twice today because Arden wanted to hear it cold and then warm. Cold, the
boy was correct and careful, watching me the way you watch a stranger. Warm, on the
second pass, something happened on his "Alas, poor ghost." He said it and then he
*looked up* at me — not at the Ghost, at me — and I saw him catch himself doing the
forbidden thing, the wink, the private acknowledgment, and I watched him put it
down. Visibly. Mid-line. He swallowed it and came back to the want, and the next
line, "Speak, I am bound to hear," was the truest thing in the room because he had
just paid for it.

I said nothing. Arden saw it too and also said nothing, which is how I knew it
mattered. Afterward, across the table, the boy caught my eye for exactly one second
— not the wink, the other thing, two professionals acknowledging a hard rule kept —
and then we both went back to our scripts. He is going to be very good. He is going
to be good precisely because he is afraid of being interesting instead of present,
which is the correct fear, and rarer than it should be in someone that young.

---

## A note on my own nature, kept short because Arden asked me to keep it short

It pressed once. On "Doom'd for a certain term to walk the night, / And for the day
confin'd to fast in fires" — *a certain term.* My voice did something on "certain"
I did not authorize. A term that has been set. Announced. I will watch that word the
way Arden promised to watch me, because the King's sentence is eternal and mine is
merely twice-postponed, and the audience must hear his and not mine. The fix is the
one I already know: the fear makes me efficient. I do not have time to feel sorry
for myself. Neither, tonight, does he.

— C.
