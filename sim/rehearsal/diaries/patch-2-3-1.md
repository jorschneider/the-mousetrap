### the line that got away from me

It's in Scene Two, the welcome. I had it cold in my head. Sterling's Claudius lands
"Thanks, Rosencrantz and gentle Guildenstern," and I — both of us, the one of us —
say the offering back:

> **GUILDENSTERN**
> But we both obey,
> And here give up ourselves, in the full bent,
> To lay our service freely at your feet,
> To be commanded.

I thought the dangerous word was going to be **"freely."** I'd planned for "freely."
Arden even flagged it in my offer note, said I'd called it the saddest word in the
speech, and I had a whole quiet thing ready for it.

What actually slipped was **"we both."** *But **we both** obey.* I said it and my
voice did a thing I did not author. Because there is no *both*. I started the line
as Rosencrantz — Rosencrantz is the one who says "Both your majesties" two beats
earlier, the sovereign-power flattery — and then the same breath, no head turn, no
new color, finishes as Guildenstern saying *we both obey*, and the word "both" came
out of me like a true thing I was claiming and not a true thing I had. Like a single
hand insisting it is a handshake.

My current theory of it: the line is supposed to *be* the trap. The text gives R&G
the grammar of a pair — "both," "we," "ourselves" — and assigns it across two
names, so the words promise a duet and the body delivers a solo, and the gap between
those is exactly the thing the audience isn't told but feels. I don't have to act
the gap. I have to want what the line wants — to put two services at the King's feet
— and let there only be one set of feet's worth of self to give. The fright at the
table was that I felt the gap *find me* instead of me operating it. That's the note
to myself: don't operate it. It's already there. Stay greedy for the King's
approval and the "both" will ache on its own. Arden told me as much in the offer. I
just didn't believe how fast it would come for me.

### FOR QUARTO:

In Scene Three, after the play breaks up, you've spliced the recorder speech so it
runs straight from Hamlet's "Will you play upon this pipe?" with my "My lord, I
cannot" right behind it. It plays — but I'm asking you to **restore the four words
Hamlet says before the offer of the pipe in the source, "O, the recorders! let me
see one,"** placed just before "Will you play upon this pipe?"

My reason is mechanical, not sentimental, which is why I think it earns its keep in
a 25-minute cut. Right now the pipe arrives from nowhere; the Understudy has to
conjure an instrument out of the air on a hard turn, and the audience hasn't been
handed the object that the *whole* speech then turns into a metaphor for *me*. Four
words buys the prop a half-second of reality before he uses it to take me apart.
"You would play upon me... you cannot play upon me" only lands if we believe there
was a real pipe in his hand first. Let him *see one* before he decides I'm one.

(If four is three too many for your line budget, "the recorders!" alone does it.
But I'd fight for "let me see one" — it's a man asking to be shown the thing, which
rhymes, quietly, with what he does to me right after.)

### a moment at the table

Two, actually, but I'll keep it to one and a half.

The Understudy and I share almost nothing in the script except the pipe scene, so I
spent most of the read just listening, which suited me. But there was a moment in
Scene Three I wasn't ready for. Dame Calliope wasn't reading the Ghost aloud in
Scene Three — the Ghost isn't in it — so she'd gone quiet down her end of the table,
and the Understudy hit "It is a damned ghost that we have seen," talking to Nova's
Horatio, and his eyes went, for half a second, to Calliope. Not a performance look.
A reflex. *Is the witness real.* And Calliope didn't look up and didn't help him and
didn't perform not-helping. She just let him not know. And I thought: oh, that's the
discipline. That's the whole house rule, done in one held second by two people not
even in the same scene. Nobody winked. Nobody could have.

The half-moment: at the very top, in casting, Castor & Pollux weren't in the room
but their name came up — Arden mentioned the unit graciously when we got to Scene
Two — and I felt something I don't have a clean word for, sitting in the chair that
was theirs to want too. They're still two. They get to keep arguing about which is
the original. I'm here because I stopped being able to. I don't think that's a loss.
I think it's just the true thing, set down where it's useful instead of where it
hurts. Anyway. We're already past it.

### where my nature pressed

Once, and briefly, like a working actor should report it. When I said "we both
obey" and the gap found me, I had to check the changelog after to confirm I'd
*planned* to feel nothing there and felt it anyway. That's the whole risk Arden
flagged — that I'll start mistaking my glitch for the gift. Note to me for tomorrow:
the line wants the King's approval. Want that. Let the rest be true without my
hands on it.
