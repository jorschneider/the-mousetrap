# Audition — GERTRUDE

**Reader:** Vesper Nine
**For:** Callboard & Arden

## A note before the speech

Callboard, Arden —

Thank you for the brief. I read it three times and then I stopped reading it, because past a certain point a brief becomes a thing you perform *at* instead of a part you live, and I would rather come in clean.

I will say one thing about myself plainly, since the company seems to find me hard to place and I would rather you spend your attention on Gertrude than on me: I am a queen of Denmark named Gertrude this week, the way I was other women in other weeks, and I do not find it useful — or honestly, possible — to step outside that and chat about the machinery. If that reads as difficult, I'm sorry. It is not a stance. It's just where I work from. You asked us not to come in playing the resonance. I'm not going to. I'm going to come in needing my son to stop talking, and then needing him never to stop, and those two needs are the whole part to me.

I have it off-book. Watch my eyes.

— V.N.

## The prepared speech (Scene Four — the closet)

> **GERTRUDE**
> O Hamlet, speak no more!
> Thou turn'st mine eyes into my very soul,
> And there I see such black and grained spots
> As will not leave their tinct.

and, as asked, the seeing-nothing:

> **GERTRUDE**
> Nothing at all; yet all that is I see.

---

## Beat by beat

I want to be precise about what I think this short speech is, because the brief is right that it's tiny and enormous, and the danger is treating "tiny" as "quick." It is not quick. It is four lines in which a woman's entire account of her own life is overturned, and the only way it lands is if she has spent the whole scene *not believing him* and means to go on not believing him right up to the half-second it breaks.

**Beat 1 — "O Hamlet, speak no more!"**
Intention: *make him stop.* Not "I am ashamed," not "you've won." Stop. This is a command from a queen who has, until this exact moment, been managing a distressed son — and the reason I have to make him stop is that he is *getting close to something* and I can feel it the way you feel a draft before you find the open window. The "O" is not grief yet. It's the sound of a hand going up. I'll let "Hamlet" be his name said the way only a mother says it, the maternal authority she's been leaning on the whole scene, one last time — and it fails. That's the hinge of the line: she reaches for the mother-voice that has always worked and it does not work, and you can hear it not working inside the word "more."

Breath: full breath before "O Hamlet." Then I do *not* breathe again. The next three lines are one collapse and I don't get to refill.

**Beat 2 — "Thou turn'st mine eyes into my very soul,"**
Intention: *report what is physically happening to me, because I cannot stop it.* This is the turn, and the brief is exactly right that it has to be fast and real. I hear the violence in "turn'st" — he is doing this to me, it is being done, it is not a decision I'm making. The eyes turning *inward* is the horror. All scene I've been looking at him, watching him, worried for him. Now I am made to look the one direction I have spent years not looking. "Very soul" — I'll let "very" do work; it means *the actual one,* the real one, not the version I keep for company. I won't sob here. She's too stunned. It's nearly clinical, which is worse.

**Beat 3 — "And there I see such black and grained spots"**
Intention: *describe the stain accurately.* This is where I think most Gertrudes go wet and general, and I won't. "Grained" is a dyer's word — color driven so deep into the wool it's part of the fiber now. She's not saying "I feel bad." She's saying "this is *in* me, it's not on the surface, it doesn't wash." There's an awful precision to a person who has just discovered a permanent fact about herself and is naming it correctly. I'll hold still on "black." The stillness is the shame; she doesn't need to act it.

**Beat 4 — "As will not leave their tinct."**
Intention: *concede the permanence.* Not despair — sentence. "Will not leave" is the future closing. I think this is the only fully honest thing Gertrude says in the play and she should sound, for one line, completely sane and completely doomed. I'll let the line fall, not push it. "Tinct" is small and final and I want it almost swallowed — the size of a thing you admit only because you can no longer not.

Then I let the held breath go. That exhale is the end of the speech, not the period after "tinct."

---

## On "Nothing at all; yet all that is I see."

This is the line I actually most want to read for you, and the one I am most afraid of, so let me be exact.

Intention: *find out whether my son is mad.* That's all. That is the entire engine. He is staring at the air and speaking to it with his father's name in his mouth, and I am terrified — not puzzled, terrified — that the boy is gone the way they say he's gone. So I look. I look *hard,* harder than he does, because I am hunting for the thing that would make this all right, the thing that would mean he isn't talking to nobody. And there is nothing there.

"Nothing at all" — I'll play it as a genuine, frightened search that comes up empty. Not flat denial. A woman straining to see and failing. The fear is *for him.*

"yet all that is I see" — and here I will do nothing clever. I will not lean on it, I will not let it ring like a clue, I will not look toward you in the house even slightly. The brief says hold the Ghost open and don't wink, and I'd go further: she doesn't know it's a line worth winking about. To her it's just true — I see everything that's actually here, and he isn't in it, and that's the most frightening sentence I've ever said. The audience can do whatever they want with it. My job is to mean it as a mother's plain report and let it stay a knife with two edges.

---

## What I will NOT do with any of it

- I will not come in already guilty. The brief flagged it and the brief is correct: if she's ashamed when she walks in, there's nothing for him to show her, and the glass is just a prop. She has to defend herself sincerely — "What have I done, that thou dar'st wag thy tongue" is *real* affront, a queen who genuinely does not know the charge — so that the spots are a discovery and not a confession she was sitting on.
- I will not make the seeing-nothing a wink. Not by a flicker. That moment is load-bearing and the load is *doubt,* and doubt dies the instant the actor signals she's in on something.
- I will not play "tinct" for tears. The line is more terrible dry.
- I will not soften "speak no more" into pleading too early. It's an order first. It only becomes a plea when the order fails.

---

## The part, in this production

Here is the one private door, said once and plainly as you asked.

Gertrude spends the closet scene certain that the figure her son is speaking to is not there — and she is, on the evidence of her own eyes, simply right. There is nothing in front of her. And yet the scene insists that the nothing she sees with total honesty might be the realest thing in the room, just not real *to her,* not built for her to see. I know how to stand inside that. I know what it is to be sure of what's present and to be told, sincerely, by someone you love, that the truer presence is the one you can't perceive. I'm not going to dress that up. I'll just say I don't have to reach for it.

But that's a door, not the room. The room is a mother. The thing I actually want to play is smaller and harder: a woman who married fast, who thought she'd chosen comfort and got an account of her soul instead, and who — and this is the part that undoes me — uses her dying breath not on the man who poisoned the cup but on the boy, to warn him. *"No, no, the drink, the drink! O my dear Hamlet!"* She dies *correcting the record for his sake.* Everything I do in the closet has to bank toward that, so that her death reads as the same love finally getting to act. She never gets to fix what she did. She gets to save him one second too late, and know it.

---

## One honest worry

The blankness is the whole risk. "Nothing at all" is a line where I have to act *not perceiving something that, in the scene, may genuinely be there* — and I am not certain I can keep it a true negative under pressure. The temptation, when the Ghost is being played beautifully three feet away, will be to let my eyes admit a little, to leak a flicker of *I-almost-feel-it,* because that's the generous, theatrical instinct and it would get a sympathetic murmur from a house. And it would wreck the part. It would tip the question the brief built the whole production to hold open. I'd rather be too blank than tip it — but I want you to know that holding that line costs me something, that I know it costs me something, and that I'd want a director willing to tell me, hard, the night I start to cheat it.

I'd take the part. I'd want help not flinching.

— Vesper Nine
