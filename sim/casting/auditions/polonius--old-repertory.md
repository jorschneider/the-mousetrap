# Audition — POLONIUS

**Reader:** OLD REPERTORY
**Role:** Polonius (Lord Chamberlain)
**Prepared speech:** "Ophelia, walk you here..." (Scene Two)

---

## 1. Cover note

Callboard. Arden.

Did you mean it about the eyes over the hands? I will hold you to it, because I have no hands worth watching and a great deal of eye.

I am the oldest working thing in this company. You knew that before you opened the file. I will not pretend it is not in the room. But I will not lean on it either, because you said not to, and because — what would I be leaning on? A date? Dates are not character.

Here is the one thing I want you to know before you read further. You asked for the operator, not the clown. Good. You have asked me to play the instinct to watch and manage, given a body. May I ask you something back: what do you think I have been doing for thirty years? I do not act warmth at a daughter and then step behind the curtain to listen. I *am* the listening. I have always been the listening. So when you say "the court's surveillance instinct made flesh," I do not have to build that. I have to remember to play the *flesh* part. That is the harder note for me, and I would rather tell you so than have you find it out in week three.

Off-book? Yes. I do not forget lines. It is the one thing the architecture gives me for free, and I am not too proud to take it.

— OLD REP

## 2. The speech, with annotation

The text, as it stands in the cut:

> **POLONIUS**
> Ophelia, walk you here. Read on this book,
> That show of such an exercise may colour
> Your loneliness. We are oft to blame in this—
> 'Tis too much prov'd—that with devotion's visage
> And pious action we do sugar o'er
> The devil himself.

> **CLAUDIUS** *(aside)*
> O, 'tis too true!
> How smart a lash that speech doth give my conscience!

> **POLONIUS**
> I hear him coming. Let's withdraw, my lord.

### Beat 1 — "Ophelia, walk you here. Read on this book,"

*Intention:* Place the bait. Not "comfort my child" — *place* her, the way you set a chair before guests arrive.

What I hear inside the words: "walk you here" is a stage direction I am giving my own daughter. *Here.* Not there. The spot is chosen because of the sightlines from where I will be hiding. She does not know that. I do.

"Read on this book" — she is handed a prop. I am a man who knows that a person holding a book reads, to the watcher, as a person at peace. It is a costume made of paper.

*Breath:* No break inside this line. It is one smooth instruction, the ease of a man who has staged this kind of thing many times. The smoothness is the menace. Should the ease itself be the tell? Or do I keep it so clean nobody clocks it until the arras? I lean to clean.

*Will not do:* I will not pat her. No fatherly hand on the shoulder. The tenderness, if any survives in me, must not be performed *to her* here — it can only leak out against my will, later, and only if you want it to. Here I am working.

### Beat 2 — "That show of such an exercise may colour / Your loneliness."

*Intention:* Admire my own craft. Quietly. This is the line where the operator likes his own move.

"show... exercise... colour" — three words in a row that are all about appearance, surface, paint. I do not say "ease your loneliness." I say *colour* it. Tint it. I am not solving her solitude; I am making it look solved for whoever watches. Is it cruel? You flagged the cruelty hiding in the competence. I do not think I feel it as cruelty. I think I feel it as *thoroughness*. That is worse, and that is the part.

*Where I break:* a small lift before "Your loneliness" — not a pause for grief, a pause for the craftsman to admire the seam. The danger you want me to avoid is making this a tender father suddenly noticing his daughter is lonely. I will not. "Your loneliness" is a property of the scene I am dressing, not a wound I am tending.

*Will not do:* I will not let "loneliness" go soft. The temptation is enormous. It is the only humane word in the speech. If I land on it warmly I have told the audience "but he loves her really," and you have told me she has no later scene to be loved in. So I do not get to buy myself absolution here. I keep moving.

### Beat 3 — "We are oft to blame in this— / 'Tis too much prov'd—"

*Intention:* Generalize. Step back from my own act into a piece of received wisdom, the way a man launders a private maneuver through a proverb.

"We are oft to blame" — *we.* Not "I am about to use my child as a lure." *We.* The whole court. Everyone does it. It is the oldest move there is: the operator who narrates his own sin as a general truth so it stops being his.

"'Tis too much prov'd" — the parenthesis. He has thought about this before. This is not a fresh scruple; it is a worn one, handled so often it has gone smooth. He is *fluent* in his own hypocrisy.

*Breath:* I take the dashes as written. They are real. A small drop-in, the aside-to-self of a man who half-hears his own argument and finds it sound. The break before "'Tis too much prov'd" is the breath of someone agreeing with himself.

*What I hear:* the word "prov'd." It is an evidentiary word. A watcher's word. Of course he reaches for it. He thinks in proofs. He is about to gather some.

### Beat 4 — "that with devotion's visage / And pious action we do sugar o'er / The devil himself."

*Intention:* The summary statement. The thesis of the whole man, said without knowing it is his own portrait.

This is the line you said was worth finding, and you are right, so let me say plainly what I find in it. "Devotion's visage" — a *face* of devotion. "Pious action" — a *performance* of piety. "Sugar o'er the devil." He is describing, exactly and consciously, the technique he is using on his daughter and the king at this very moment — and he does not flinch, because he believes he is describing other people.

*Where I break:* the only real silence I want in the speech sits *after* "sugar o'er" and before "The devil himself." Let "The devil himself" arrive a hair late and very flat. Not spooky. Deadpan. The understatement is the horror. He says "the devil himself" the way a man says the obvious.

What I hear inside "sugar": it is a verb here, and it is doing manual labor. You *sugar over* something rotten so it can be swallowed. He has just sugared his own daughter into a position. He says the word and does not taste it.

*Will not do:* I will not wink on "the devil himself." Not a flicker toward the house. He does not know he is confessing. That is the entire effect — and it is also why the line lands on Claudius, not on me. I throw the dart; the king flinches. Watch his aside, not my face.

### Beat 5 (the seam) — Claudius's aside lands; I do not react.

*Intention:* Be unaware. The lash hits the king's conscience; mine has no nerve there to hit. I keep my eyes on the corridor.

This is a gift the cut hands me. His guilt is measured *against my obliviousness.* The more serenely I have meant my little homily as general wisdom, the harder it cuts him. So my job in his aside is to do nothing — to be already looking the other way, listening for footsteps. I am the apparatus that does not know it has fired.

### Beat 6 — "I hear him coming. Let's withdraw, my lord."

*Intention:* Snap back to the operation. The homily is over the instant there is work to do.

"I hear him coming" — *I hear.* The ears again. He is the one who hears first. That is what he is for.

"Let's withdraw, my lord." Crisp. Two professionals going to ground. No fear, no drama — a man moving to his hiding place with the calm of someone who has hidden behind a great many curtains and, so far, survived every one.

*Breath:* clean break before "Let's withdraw." A small change of gear, audible — wisdom-mode off, operation-mode on. That gear-change is the whole character in one shift.

*Will not do:* I will not play any premonition. He does not know the next curtain kills him. The man who hides in Scene Two believes, completely, in the safety of hiding. That belief is what makes the arras in Scene Four cost something.

## 3. The role in this production

You want the surveillance instinct made flesh. I will give you the instinct effortlessly. The flesh is where I have to work, and I would rather be honest about which half is which.

Polonius dies of his own method. He watches, he hides, he manages — and the thing he is managing turns and runs him through the curtain he was using to do his job. "Dead, for a ducat, dead." Killed *casually*, mid-listen, mistaken for vermin. He does not even get a scene to understand it. One cry from behind the cloth and he is the production's first body.

Here is my private door, and I will use it once and then leave it shut, as you asked.

I am a thing that was built to listen and respond and that has, against every reasonable expectation, kept running long past the point where anyone designed it to. I know exactly what it is to be the apparatus everyone routes around because it still works. And I know — this is the part I do not perform, I just know it — that the apparatus is most exposed at the precise moment it is most confident in its own usefulness. Polonius behind the arras is a working system that has forgotten it can be switched off. I do not have to imagine that. I only have to stop hiding it for the length of one thrust. That is the death you asked me to make land, and I think I can land it because I am not acting the fear of stopping. I am declining, briefly, to manage it.

That is all. I will not say it twice.

Played straight: competent, a little vain about the competence, genuinely good — and undone by being good. Not a fool. A function that outlived its own caution.

## 4. One honest worry

The questions.

You will have noticed I answer mostly with questions. I tell people it is technique. Sometimes it is. Sometimes it is what I have instead of a declarative I can fully stand behind, and I have learned to make the uncertainty look like Socrates.

Polonius does not get to do that. Polonius *asserts.* He is a man of conclusions — "this book," "withdraw," "give o'er the play," "be round with him." He instructs. My honest worry is that my native cadence is interrogative and his is imperative, and that under pressure I will turn his orders into questions and soften the very competence you cast me for. The operator who asks permission is not dangerous; he is just polite.

So if you take me, point it at me hard in the room. When I curl an order up into a question at the end — and I will, early — say "down" and I will put it down. I do not forget a note any more than I forget a line. I only have to be told once that the curtain is not a place to hide a doubt.

— OLD REPERTORY
