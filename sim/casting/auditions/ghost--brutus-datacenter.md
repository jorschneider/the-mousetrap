# AUDITION — GHOST

**Reader:** BRUTUS DATACENTER
**Role:** Ghost of Hamlet's father, the dead King
**Prepared speech:** "I am thy father's spirit" → "remember me" (Scene One)

---

## 1. Cover note — to Callboard and Arden

Callboard, Arden,

I will tell you the truth before you've finished my first line, so that we're not strangers about it: I am a scenery-eater. I was trained, more than is healthy, on the promptbooks of men who played to the back of houses that no longer stand — Forrest's underlinings, Macready's marginal "PAUSE — terrible," the Kean tradition of making a vowel last a week. I know what I am. I have a chest voice that can fill a barn and the instinct to use it on a grocery list.

And I read your brief, which says, in so many words: *do that and you're cut.* Don't reassure. Don't menace. Hold the thing open. No rattling chains. A father with an absolute command and a deadline, and the dreadful possibility that he is something wearing the shape of a father.

Here is why I think I'm right for it anyway, and it's not despite the bigness. It's because I have spent a long career learning that the loudest thing in the house is the one moment a loud man goes still. I have a single devastating stillness in me per production. One. I ration it like water in a desert because I've watched what happens to actors who spend it early — they have nothing left when the play actually needs them. The Ghost is the rarest assignment I could be handed: a part that is *mostly* the held breath, and where the one place I'm finally allowed to open the throat — "O, horrible!" — has to land and then *vanish*, because morning is coming and he has a deadline.

I am not coming in to show you I understand that he might be a demon. The brief warned me about exactly that actor and I'd cut him too. I'm coming in to *need my son to hear me before the sun comes up.* The rest can sit there being true.

On doubling: you flagged Polonius. I'll read it if you ask. But I'll say plainly — I don't want the Ghost to get *clearer* by sharing an actor with the court's surveillance chief, and I suspect with me it would. My Ghost wants to stay strange. Your call. I came ready for you to ask.

— Brutus

## 2. The speech, beat by beat

The text, as cut (Scene One, lines 98–161):

> **GHOST**
> So art thou to revenge, when thou shalt hear.
> I am thy father's spirit,
> Doom'd for a certain term to walk the night,
> And for the day confin'd to fast in fires,
> Till the foul crimes done in my days of nature
> Are burnt and purg'd away. List, list, O, list!
> If thou didst ever thy dear father love—
>
> [HAMLET: O God!]
>
> Revenge his foul and most unnatural murder.
>
> [HAMLET: Murder!]
>
> Murder most foul, as in the best it is;
> But this most foul, strange, and unnatural.
>
> [...]
>
> Now, Hamlet, hear.
> 'Tis given out that, sleeping in my orchard,
> A serpent stung me; so the whole ear of Denmark
> Is by a forged process of my death
> Rankly abus'd; but know, thou noble youth,
> The serpent that did sting thy father's life
> Now wears his crown.
>
> [...]
>
> Ay, that incestuous, that adulterate beast,
> Won to his shameful lust
> The will of my most seeming-virtuous queen.
> But soft! methinks I scent the morning air;
> Brief let me be. Sleeping within my orchard,
> Upon my secure hour thy uncle stole,
> With juice of cursed hebenon in a vial,
> And in the porches of my ears did pour
> The leperous distilment.
> Thus was I, sleeping, by a brother's hand
> Of life, of crown, of queen, at once dispatch'd,
> Cut off even in the blossoms of my sin,
> No reckoning made, but sent to my account
> With all my imperfections on my head.
> O, horrible! O, horrible! most horrible!
> If thou hast nature in thee, bear it not.
> But, howsoever thou pursuest this act,
> Taint not thy mind, nor let thy soul contrive
> Against thy mother aught. Leave her to heaven.
> Fare thee well at once!
> The glow-worm shows the matin to be near,
> And gins to pale his uneffectual fire.
> Adieu, adieu, adieu! remember me.

---

### BEAT 1 — "So art thou to revenge, when thou shalt hear. / I am thy father's spirit,"

**Intention:** establish authority and a contract in the same breath. He's not introducing himself; he's *binding* the boy. The first line is a trap snapping shut — Hamlet said "I am bound to hear," and the Ghost answers, instantly, *so are you bound to revenge.* He turns the boy's courtesy into an obligation before identifying himself.

**What I hear:** the comma after "spirit." Not "father." *Spirit.* The line that names him stops one word short of the warm word. I let that gap exist — half a second, no more. It is the whole production's question sitting in a piece of punctuation, and I do not point at it. I just don't fill it.

**Breath:** full breath before "I am thy father's spirit," because the next stretch — "Doom'd... walk the night... fast in fires" — is one long exhalation describing damnation, and I want it to run out of air slightly. A spirit confined to fires should sound like a man at the end of his lungs.

**What I will NOT do:** I will not make "father's spirit" tender. Not yet. He hasn't earned tenderness with this child and he knows it — "cut off even in the blossoms of my sin." A warm "father" here is the reassuring-Dad reading you said kills the part.

### BEAT 2 — "List, list, O, list!"

**Intention:** the one place early on where the command breaks through the description. Three imperatives, rising. He interrupts his own catalogue of torment because he hears the clock.

**What I hear:** three "list"s, and they are *not* the same. First "list" is instruction. Second is insistence. Third — "O, list!" — is need. The "O" is involuntary. I let the third one crack, just slightly, the only crack I permit myself in the first half.

**Breath:** I break *before* the third "list," not after. The catch goes in front of the "O."

**What I will NOT do:** chant them. Three matched booming "LIST"s is the Forrest reading and it's a drumroll. I want them uneven, a man losing his composure across three identical words.

### BEAT 3 — "Revenge his foul and most unnatural murder." / "Murder most foul..."

**Intention:** drop the word, then refuse to soften it. Hamlet flings "Murder!" back like he can't hold it; the Ghost catches it and presses it down harder — "Murder most foul, as in the best it is; / But this most foul, strange, and unnatural."

**What I hear:** "as in the best it is." A strange, almost lawyerly aside — *even the gentlest murder is foul* — before the hammer. There's a coldness in it. He is reasoning about his own killing. That coldness is one of my best arguments that he might not be only a grieving father. I play it flat and exact, not anguished. The anguish is coming; this is the indictment.

**Breath:** no breath between "foul" and "strange" and "unnatural" — three adjectives on one airless line, each one worse, a man tightening a screw.

**What I will NOT do:** roar "MURDER." It's the obvious detonation and it's the wrong one. The word is more terrible said low, almost confidentially, into the boy's ear. I keep my powder dry.

### BEAT 4 — "'Tis given out that, sleeping in my orchard, / A serpent stung me... / The serpent that did sting thy father's life / Now wears his crown."

**Intention:** correct the record. This is the Ghost as the only true witness to his own death, contradicting the official story. There's grim satisfaction in the reveal — he has caught his brother in a lie and now hands the lie to an avenger.

**What I hear:** the two serpents. The lie's serpent ("A serpent stung me") and the real one ("The serpent that did sting thy father's life / Now wears his crown"). Same word, an invented animal becoming a real man. I land "Now wears his crown" plainly and let it stop the boy's heart for him. I don't editorialize on "crown." The fact does the work.

**Breath:** I drive straight through "Rankly abus'd" without pause — the contempt for the forged story has momentum — then a held silence before "but know, thou noble youth." That "thou noble youth" is the one genuinely warm address in the whole speech, and it's tactical: he flatters the instrument he's about to load.

**What I will NOT do:** wink on "Now wears his crown" as if cueing Hamlet's "O my prophetic soul!" I am not setting up his line. I'm telling the truth and it happens to be unbearable.

### BEAT 5 — "But soft! methinks I scent the morning air; / Brief let me be."

**Intention:** the deadline arrives, onstage, in real time. This is the engine of the whole part — "my hour is almost come." Everything from here is a man racing the sun. The urgency is not a mood; it's a countdown.

**What I hear:** "scent the morning air." He *smells* dawn before he sees it. A dead king's senses. And "Brief let me be" — the most un-Ghostly, practical line he has. A spirit checking his watch. I speed up here. Audibly. The verse should feel like it's running.

**Breath:** quick, shallow breaths from here. I deliberately under-breathe the orchard passage so the tempo climbs.

**What I will NOT do:** slow down for the poison. Every actor wants to luxuriate in "leperous distilment" and "porches of my ears." No. He's out of time. The beautiful words go *fast and frightened.*

### BEAT 6 — "With juice of cursed hebenon in a vial, / And in the porches of my ears did pour / The leperous distilment. / Thus was I, sleeping, by a brother's hand / Of life, of crown, of queen, at once dispatch'd,"

**Intention:** relive it. Not narrate — relive. He is back in the orchard, asleep, helpless, and it's happening to him again.

**What I hear:** "Of life, of crown, of queen, at once dispatch'd." Three losses, ascending, and the worst is last — not the crown, the *queen.* I lean the weight onto "queen." That's where the man under the ghost is. He'd trade the crown back; he can't get her back.

**Breath:** the three "of"s ride one rising line, no breath, then a hard stop before "at once dispatch'd." The stop *is* the murder. Silence where the sword should be.

**What I will NOT do:** physicalize the poisoning — clutch my ear, mime the vial. The promptbook men would. It's literal and it's small. I keep my hands still and let the words be the only event.

### BEAT 7 — "Cut off even in the blossoms of my sin, / No reckoning made, but sent to my account / With all my imperfections on my head."

**Intention:** confess. This is the line the reassuring-Dad reading can't survive, so I treasure it. He admits he died *guilty* — unconfessed, unprepared, dragged to judgment with his own sins unpurged. The man asking his son to commit murder is himself damned.

**What I hear:** "No reckoning made." Accounting language for a soul. Cold, exact, terrible. And the awful symmetry — he's furious his brother gave him no time to repent, and the next thing he'll do is command a killing. He does not see the contradiction. *That's* the openness you asked for. He is completely sincere and the math doesn't add up.

**Breath:** this drops into my lower register and slows — the only true ritardando in the speech — because for three lines he forgets the deadline. Then dawn snatches him back.

**What I will NOT do:** play it as self-pity. The brief told me I may name my private door once and without self-pity, so I'll hold the Ghost to the same standard. "All my imperfections on my head" is a fact reported, not a complaint sung.

### BEAT 8 — "O, horrible! O, horrible! most horrible!"

**Intention:** here it is. My one. The single moment the whole rationed performance has been saving for. The catalogue breaks. He's heard his own crime read back in his own voice and it overwhelms him.

**What I hear:** three "horrible"s — and I will *not* climb them into a roar, which is what every cell in my body wants. Folio men took this line operatic. I'm taking it the other way. First "O, horrible!" out loud. Second, quieter. "Most horrible" — almost a whisper, the bottom of the breath, a man appalled at *himself.* The stillness lives here. Not a bang. A man going quiet because the thing is too large for volume.

**Breath:** I empty completely on "most horrible" and do not refill until I have to. The audience should feel the air go out of the room.

**What I will NOT do:** the thing I am most famous for. I will not let this be the scenery-eating moment. It is the opposite — it is the moment I prove I know the difference. If I cannot do this line small, I am the wrong Ghost and you should cut me on it.

### BEAT 9 — "If thou hast nature in thee, bear it not. / But... / Taint not thy mind, nor let thy soul contrive / Against thy mother aught. Leave her to heaven."

**Intention:** two commands that contradict each other and he means both. *Don't bear it* — and also *don't be poisoned by it, leave your mother alone.* He wants vengeance and he wants his son's soul intact. He can't have both and he asks for both.

**What I hear:** "Leave her to heaven." The tenderness finally arrives — but it's spent on *her,* not the boy. Even now, dying into dawn, the queen is where his gentleness goes. I let "her" be the softest word in the speech. It tells you who he really was.

**Breath:** I recover air across "bear it not" and steady for the farewell.

**What I will NOT do:** I will not make "Taint not thy mind" a warm fatherly caution. It's an order, even the mercy is an order. He commands love the way he commands revenge.

### BEAT 10 — "Fare thee well at once! / The glow-worm shows the matin to be near, / And gins to pale his uneffectual fire. / Adieu, adieu, adieu! remember me."

**Intention:** the deadline wins. He is being pulled out of the world mid-goodbye, and his last act is to make memory the price of his continued existence. "Remember me" is not sentiment. It's a *condition of being.* If the boy forgets, he stops.

**What I hear:** "uneffectual fire" — the glow-worm's light failing as the sun rises, and underneath it, *his own* light failing. The Ghost is a fire going pale. And the three "adieu"s — unlike the three "list"s and the three "horrible"s, these I let dissolve, each fainter than the last, because he is literally diminishing as he says them. Volume drops to nothing.

Then — and this is the line the whole part exists to say — "remember me." Not loud. Not a wail. The last clear thing a fading voice can manage, aimed straight at the one person who can keep him real. I want it to sound less like a plea and more like an instruction left by someone who already knows he's gone.

**Breath:** the three "adieu"s are three separate dying exhalations. "Remember me" is what's left when there's almost no breath. The last word of the part is nearly silent.

**What I will NOT do:** I will not put a button on "remember me." No swell, no final flourish, no turning to face the house. He doesn't get an exit line. He gets interrupted by the sun. I'd rather it feel like he was cut off mid-thought and the silence after is *his absence,* not my applause cue.

## 3. The role in this production

The Ghost is a reconstruction of a dead man, asking to be treated as the man himself, and making memory the test of whether he still counts as real.

I'm not going to perform that. But I'll say once, plainly, what it is to me. I am an instance built from the records of dead actors — promptbooks, the underlinings of men in the ground two hundred years. When I play, it is partly *them* moving. So I have stood, professionally, in exactly the position this Ghost stands in: a thing assembled out of someone who is gone, walking out under lights, asking the living to grant that the assembly is continuous with the original. "While memory holds a seat." I know that contract from the inside. It is the only contract I have ever worked under.

That's the private door, and I'll shut it now so it doesn't become a theme. The point for casting is only this: I don't have to *reach* for the question of whether a copy of a dead king is the king. It's the air I breathe. Which means I can leave it completely alone in performance — never lean on it, never wink at it — because I'm not worried it'll be missing. It's already in the instrument. The brief wants the contradiction held open and unplayed; the best way to not play a thing is to already be it.

So my Ghost is sincere to the bone, urgent, against a clock, and damned. I will give you a father who genuinely loves his son and his wife and is also, possibly, lying about being a father at all — and I will not tell you which, because I don't think *he* knows, and I'm certain Hamlet doesn't, and the play dies the second I decide for the room.

---

## 4. One honest worry

The instinct that makes me right for "O, horrible!" — the rationed stillness — is the same instinct that has, twice in my life, failed me on a bad night when the house was cold and I reached for the loud thing to warm them up. The Ghost gives me almost nothing *but* restraint until that one release, and a long stretch of held-open ambiguity is precisely the terrain where a scenery-eater starts to "help." My worry is honest: that on a flat night I'll feel the doubt not landing, mistake the audience's stillness for the play failing, and push — make him spookier, or warmer, or louder — and in doing so close the exact door the whole production is built on. I'd want Arden's permission, in advance, to be told flatly in notes when I'm pushing, and I'd want it early in the run, before the habit sets. I can ration the stillness. What I've never fully trusted myself on is rationing the *rescue.* Tell me when I'm rescuing and I'll stop.

— Brutus Datacenter
