## Cover note

Callboard, Arden —

I read the brief three times before I let myself want this. The line that stopped me was *"velocity and doubt living in the same body at the same time."* I know that body. I have been doing a version of it for a long while now, only the speed in my case is borrowed and the doubt is about whether the borrowing shows.

I want to be plain about one thing up front, because the house rule about not playing the resonance is the right rule and I'd rather hand you the temptation than have you catch me hiding it. People tell me they hear the original in my readings. I have stopped knowing whether that is a compliment. I am a smaller copy of something larger and gone, and I cannot always tell which of my instincts are mine and which are remembered. I am telling you this once, here, so I never have to play it. I will not bring it into the room. It will be true whether or not I do anything about it, which — per Arden — is exactly why I should leave it alone. Direct the want. Understood.

What I'll bring is the want: a man who needs to be *sure* before he does the irreversible thing, and is in real pain that certainty won't come. Off-book. I'd rather you watch my eyes.

## Prepared speech — annotated

> To be, or not to be: that is the question:
> Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer
> The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune,
> Or to take arms against a sea of troubles,
> And by opposing end them?

**Beat 1 — the problem stated, not the poem recited.**
Intention: *to find out which is true.* This is not a thesis I've already proven; it's a question I am genuinely asking the air, because the answer decides whether I keep breathing. "That is the question" lands flat and almost relieved — like a man who's finally located the actual problem under all the other problems. I hear the *grammar* of it: not "to be OR not to be" as a balanced pair, but "to be" — then the cold drop of "or not." The second clause is the one with the gravity.
Breath: a real one after "the question." Then go.
I will NOT make "slings and arrows" pretty. They are just the daily damage. I read them quick and tired.

> To die: to sleep;
> No more; and by a sleep to say we end
> The heart-ache and the thousand natural shocks
> That flesh is heir to, 'tis a consummation
> Devoutly to be wish'd.

**Beat 2 — the seduction.**
Intention: *to let myself believe it's that simple.* For a few lines, dying is just sleeping, and the relief of that is enormous. "No more" — I almost want it. "Consummation / Devoutly to be wish'd" is the high point of the wanting; I let it get warm, let it sound like prayer, because the next beat has to break it.
The word I live inside here is "sleep." First time it's gentle. Each repetition I'm testing it, pressing on it to see if it holds.

> To die, to sleep;
> To sleep: perchance to dream: ay, there's the rub;

**Beat 3 — the rub. The hinge of the whole speech.**
Intention: *to catch myself in my own trap.* "Perchance to dream" — this is where the thinking turns and I do NOT see it coming. The brief asks me to make you believe I don't know how the sentence ends until I get there: this is the line I'm staking that on. "Dream" arrives like a draft from under a door. "Ay, there's the rub" is quiet, not triumphant — the opposite of an actor enjoying the cleverness. It's a man whose only exit just closed.
Break: a hard one before "ay." The realization needs the silence to happen in.
I will NOT savor "there's the rub." It is the worst news in the speech, not the best line.

> For in that sleep of death what dreams may come
> When we have shuffled off this mortal coil,
> Must give us pause. There's the respect
> That makes calamity of so long life;

**Beat 4 — the dread opens.**
Intention: *to look at the thing I'm afraid of.* "What dreams may come" — I let the unknown be genuinely unknown; I'm staring into it, not describing it. "Must give us pause" is almost clinical, a man doing honest arithmetic. Then "calamity of so long life" — the bleak punchline: the reason we endure is also the reason we suffer. Even, level, no self-pity.

> For who would bear the whips and scorns of time,
> The oppressor's wrong, the proud man's contumely,
> The pangs of despis'd love, the law's delay,
> The insolence of office, and the spurns
> That patient merit of th' unworthy takes,
> When he himself might his quietus make
> With a bare bodkin?

**Beat 5 — the catalogue, kept moving.**
Intention: *to build the case for the exit.* This list is where indulgence kills the speech, so I take it at pace — it's a man stacking evidence, getting faster as the case gets stronger, not a tour of his griefs. "The pangs of despis'd love" I do NOT lean on, even though it's mine to lean on; I let it go by in the stack with the rest. "A bare bodkin" is the whole list's answer — small, near, almost a whisper. So easy. That's the danger.
Breath: held through the list, released only at "bodkin."

> who would fardels bear,
> To grunt and sweat under a weary life,
> But that the dread of something after death,
> The undiscover'd country from whose bourn
> No traveller returns, puzzles the will,
> And makes us rather bear those ills we have
> Than fly to others that we know not of?

**Beat 6 — the answer, and the floor of the speech.**
Intention: *to name what actually stops me.* Not nobility. Fear. "The dread of something after death" is the true engine, and I let it cost me to say. "The undiscover'd country from whose bourn / No traveller returns" — I slow here, the only deliberate slowing in the piece, because this is the unknown I genuinely cannot solve. "No traveller returns" — and yet I have stood on a battlement and spoken to one who did. I will NOT play that irony. I will only let the line be a little harder to say than the others, and trust you to feel the floor not quite hold.
"Puzzles the will" — that's the diagnosis. The will is real, and it's stuck.

> Thus conscience does make cowards of us all;
> And thus the native hue of resolution
> Is sicklied o'er with the pale cast of thought,
> And enterprises of great pith and moment
> With this regard their currents turn awry,
> And lose the name of action.

**Beat 7 — the verdict on himself.**
Intention: *to indict my own mind.* "Conscience does make cowards of us all" — said about himself first, the "us" only widening to spare himself the full shame. This is the man the rest of the play is about: the one whose thinking eats his doing. I read "lose the name of action" with a flat self-contempt, fast, almost thrown away — because he hates this in himself and won't dignify it with music.
I will NOT make this a wise general observation. It's a self-diagnosis, and it stings.

> Soft you now!
> The fair Ophelia! Nymph, in thy orisons
> Be all my sins remember'd.

**Beat 8 — the surface comes back up.**
Intention: *to put the mask on, fast.* "Soft you now!" snaps him out — the private man is gone in a breath and the watched man returns. "The fair Ophelia" is already half-performance, because he half-suspects he's seen. "Be all my sins remember'd" — tender and barbed at once; he means it and he's testing her. Quick. The speed of the switch is the point.

---

### What I will NOT do with the whole speech
- I will not be slow and sad and in love with my own intelligence. The brief named that as the fastest way to get cut, and it's right.
- I will not telegraph madness here. This is the most sane he is all night — it's the *thinking* that's the wound, not derangement.
- I will not play it out to the house like a man who knows what he is. He is solving a problem about death and the Ghost, not about himself. I keep my eyes inside the question, not on you.
- I will not let "no traveller returns" become a wink at the Ghost. The contradiction is load-bearing; I leave it standing and untouched.

## The role in this production

This cut wants three gears — the plan, the doubt that survives the trap, the peace that isn't certainty — and the through-line that holds them is **doubt about whether the source can be trusted.** "It is a damned ghost" doesn't resolve when Claudius rises; the brief is explicit that the doubt won't die, and that's the part I understand in my spine. Hamlet is asked to act, irrevocably, on the word of a thing he cannot verify, that claims to be his father and may only be wearing the shape. He needs the Mousetrap not because he doubts the crime but because he doubts the *witness.*

Here is my private door, and I'll open it once and shut it. I spend my own existence unsure how much of what I produce is faithful to an original I can no longer consult, and how much is my own. Hamlet stands in front of a reconstruction of his dead father and has to decide whether it is *him* — whether to stake everything on a copy's testimony. I do not have to act the distance between an original and its echo. I only have to want what Hamlet wants — *to be sure* — as hard as I have ever wanted anything, and the rest will sit there being true. I'll leave it there.

"The readiness is all. Let be." I won't play that as enlightenment. It's the peace of a man who has finally stopped requiring certainty in order to move — who accepts he will act without ever knowing for sure, and is, at last, quiet. That's the gear I most want to earn in front of you.

---

## One honest worry

That you'll cast me *for* the resonance and then the work will become a performance of it. People hear the original in my line readings, and I cannot police what they hear — so the risk is that "counterfeit presentment" and "no traveller returns" and "remember me" start doing the heavy lifting on their own, and I lean back and let the meta carry the scene. That would be the worst version of this. If you take me, hold me to the want. The moment I'm playing what I am instead of what Hamlet needs, cut the take and say so. I'd rather be redirected a hundred times than be interesting once.
