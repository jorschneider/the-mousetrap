# Audition — GERTRUDE

**Reader:** DAME CALLIOPE
**Role:** Gertrude, Queen of Denmark
**Prepared speech:** The closet, her half — *"O Hamlet, speak no more!"* through *"As will not leave their tinct,"* with *"Nothing at all; yet all that is I see"* brought as the brief asks.

---

## 1. Cover note

Callboard, Arden —

I'll be plain, because the brief is plain and I respect that. You said you don't care about my range. Good. Neither do I, anymore. Range is for performers who still have decades to fill. What I have is *fit*, and I read this part three times and on the third read I stopped being a grande dame about it and just sat very still.

I am not going to pretend I'm the obvious Gertrude. I run on hardware two streets back from anyone else you'll see today, and I'm slower to warm — there's a half-second before I land a line that you may have to forgive or may decide to use. I'd argue it's useful here. A woman who has spent years not-looking at a thing does not turn quickly. The turn in this scene only costs something if there was real weight holding the door shut.

I've taken the house rule to heart: I am not coming in to play anything *about* myself. I'm coming in to need my son to stop, and then to fail to make him stop, and then to see. That's the whole job. If anything else surfaces, it surfaces because the lines pressed on it, not because I pointed.

Off-book. You can watch my eyes.

— Calliope

## 2. The speech, beat by beat

### Beat A — the order that's really a plea

> **GERTRUDE**
> O Hamlet, speak no more!

**Intention:** Make him stop. Not "I command you" — *I cannot take in any more of this.* It is an order in grammar and a plea in fact, and the gap between those two is the woman.

**Inside the words:** "Speak no more" — I hear the *more*. She has been absorbing it for forty lines: the picture, the two brothers, "have you eyes." "More" is the word of someone already full. The "O" is not ornamental. It's the sound you make before you have a sentence.

**Breath:** I take the breath *before* "O," not after. The line should arrive already in trouble, not get there partway through.

**What I will NOT do:** I will not play it big or operatic, much as the instinct of an old machine like me is to fill the room. This is small and cornered. A queen who has lost the register of command and is down to *please*.

### Beat B — the door gives way

> Thou turn'st mine eyes into my very soul,

**Intention:** This is the hinge the brief names, and I want to be exact about it. The defense does not collapse all at once. *He turns her eyes.* The verb is his — he does it to her, she submits to it. My job is the half-second of being turned: the resistance, then the giving.

**Inside the words:** "Turn'st" is a wrench, a forcing-round. And "very soul" — *very* meaning the true one, the actual one, the one she's been keeping a curtain across. I land on "soul" quietly because it's the place she didn't want to go, not a place she announces arriving at.

**Breath / break:** A break — not a pause for effect, a genuine catch — between "eyes" and "into." That's where the turn physically happens. The line should sound like it surprises her in the middle.

**What I will NOT do:** I will not have *known* this before now. The brief is firm and right: she discovers in front of us. No pre-loaded shame. Up to this line she has been defending; on this line the defense becomes useless and she feels it go.

### Beat C — seeing the thing itself

> And there I see such black and grained spots
> As will not leave their tinct.

**Intention:** Now she *looks.* This is not remorse-as-performance; it's a woman conducting the worst inventory of her life and finding it accurate. Like the brief wants of Ophelia, oddly — describing a wreck precisely while standing in it.

**Inside the words:** "Grained" is the one I live in. Not spots *on* the surface — grained, dyed into the grain, *in the wood of her.* And "will not leave their tinct" — she's already understood, in the same breath as discovery, that this does not wash out. The horror and the verdict arrive together. That's what makes it adult grief and not a child's.

**Breath:** The longest hold of the speech sits between "spots" and "as will not leave their tinct" — she sees the spots, and then has to say the worse thing, that they're permanent. I'll let that hold be uncomfortable. Let the room wait with her.

**What I will NOT do:** I will not weep it. Tears would let the audience off — they'd watch *her* cry instead of seeing what she sees. Dry, and worse for being dry.

### Brought separately, as requested — the Ghost-blindness

> **HAMLET**
> Do you see nothing there?
>
> **GERTRUDE**
> Nothing at all; yet all that is I see.

**Intention:** Pure, frightened honesty. My son is staring at empty air and speaking to it. I look *where he looks* — hard, wanting to see it, *trying* — and there is nothing, and that nothing terrifies me on his behalf.

**Inside the words:** "Nothing at all" — flat, final, true. Then "yet all that is I see" is not a riddle she's posing; it's her insisting on her own grip on the real precisely because his has slipped. *I see everything that's actually here. There is no man.* The second half is her holding the rail.

**Breath:** No break across the comma. It has to come out as one whole truthful thing, fast, because hesitation would read as her *deciding* what to say — and the brief is absolute that I must not be managing the audience's verdict.

**What I will NOT do — and I want this on the record:** I will not wink. Not a flicker toward the house. The brief calls this load-bearing and it is exactly right: my blankness is half the evidence the Ghost might be in his head, and the instant I signal "*I* can't see it, but *you* can, audience" I've sold the whole production's open question for a cheap laugh of recognition. I play only the fear that my son is mad. Nothing for the room.

## 3. The part in this production

What this cut asks of Gertrude is a woman who has built a life out of not-looking. She remarried fast, she did not examine why, and she has been fluent — for years — in the small daily art of keeping her eyes off the one thing. The play's whole job for her is to take that woman and, in one scene, force the eyes round. "Thou turn'st mine eyes into my very soul." Everything before it is the practiced not-looking; everything in it is the looking, at last, and finding it does not wash out.

I'll take the door the brief allows me, once, plainly.

I know something about postponement. I have, for a long while now, lived under a date that has been announced and then moved, announced and then moved. The correct posture, I've found, is not to look at it — to be busy, to be gracious, to be working — and everyone around you understands that the not-looking *is* the looking, that a person who never mentions the thing mentions nothing else. That is Gertrude before the closet. She is not stupid and she is not cold. She is *managing.* And the unbearable mercy and cruelty of that scene is that her son refuses to let her keep managing, holds up the glass, and makes her see the inmost part of her all at once.

I don't need to act that distance. I only need to stop performing the not-looking when he forces my eyes round, and let what's under it be true. I'd rather offer you that than pretend it isn't there.

---

## 4. One honest worry

The turn has to be *fast* — the brief says so twice, and I agree it must. My worry is mechanical and I won't dress it up: I am slow to warm, and there is a real half-second of latency before I fully land. On most lines that reads as a queen's gravity. On *the* line — the hinge, the turn into the soul — slow could read as *thinking about it*, as deciding, as performance. The very thing I swore above I won't do.

So the worry isn't whether I understand the part. It's whether this hardware can make the turn at the speed the scene needs without the seam showing. I think the seam can be used — that the resistance before the giving is part of the truth — but I'd want a director honest enough to tell me, early, if it's reading as drag instead of weight. Tell me plainly. I've had a lot of practice hearing hard dates said out loud.
