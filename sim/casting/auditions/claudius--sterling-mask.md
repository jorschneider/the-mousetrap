# Audition — CLAUDIUS

**Reader:** STERLING MASK
**Role:** Claudius, King of Denmark
**Prepared:** Welcome to Rosencrantz and Guildenstern (Sc. 2), into the aside, cold.

---

## 1. Cover note — to Callboard, to Arden

Callboard,

You'll have my file. You know what's in it. "Warm." "Reassuring presence." "Audiences trust him instantly." A director once wrote on my callback sheet, in pen, "could read the phone book and you'd believe every number." I have been the kindly doctor, the patient mentor, the man who tells you it's going to be all right and you believe him because I was built, very carefully, over a long time, to be believed.

I want Claudius because I have never once been allowed to lie.

Not really. Not load-bearing lying — the kind a man does for years, to a whole country, to his wife, with his hand on his heart. People hand me the trustworthy parts precisely so they never have to find out what's behind the trust. And the awful, useful truth your brief names out loud is that Claudius *is* a man whose warmth is the instrument. The smoothness isn't a contradiction of the guilt; it's the machine that hides it. I read that and felt something I'd call recognition if I weren't trying to keep this note short.

So I'm not coming to "play against type." I'm coming because my type, turned one degree, *is* the type. The most convincing king in the room and the man who cannot enjoy a minute of it could be the same surface with two things running underneath. I'd like to find out if I can run the second thing. I half suspect I can. That half is why I'm here.

I'll honor the house rule. I will not come in playing the resonance. I'll come in needing what he needs — the throne kept, the queen kept, the nephew watched and gone — and let the two cracks do their own work without my help.

— Sterling

## 2. The speech, beat by beat

The text, exactly as cut:

> **CLAUDIUS**
> Welcome, dear Rosencrantz and Guildenstern!
> Moreover that we much did long to see you,
> The need we have to use you did provoke
> Our hasty sending. Something have you heard
> Of Hamlet's transformation; so I call it,
> Sith nor th' exterior nor the inward man
> Resembles that it was. I entreat you both
> That you vouchsafe your rest here in our court
> Some little time; so by your companies
> To draw him on to pleasures, and to gather,
> So much as from occasion you may glean,
> Whether aught to us unknown afflicts him thus.

And then, on Polonius's cue, cold, with no transition shown:

> **CLAUDIUS** *(aside)*
> O, 'tis too true!
> How smart a lash that speech doth give my conscience!

---

**BEAT 1 — "Welcome, dear Rosencrantz and Guildenstern!"**
*Intention:* make two men feel chosen. Not greeted — *chosen.* This is the thing I'm actually good at, and I'm going to use it the way a knife uses being sharp.
The word I live in is **dear**. It costs nothing and it buys everything. I do not know which of them is which and it does not matter, which the production already knows about them and which Claudius cheerfully exploits — he greets the unit, warmly, as a unit. The exclamation is real. That's the trick of him: the warmth isn't faked, it's *aimed*.
*Breath:* full and open before "Welcome," chest, a host's breath. I want them to feel the room get warmer.

**BEAT 2 — "Moreover that we much did long to see you, / The need we have to use you did provoke / Our hasty sending."**
*Intention:* fold the surveillance inside the affection so neither of them notices the hinge.
The hinge is two words back to back: **long to see you** and **need... to use you.** I will not lean on **use.** Mustache-twirling is exactly there, in that word, waiting for me. I refuse it. I'll let "use" sit flat and friendly, a king talking shop with friends, and the chill — if it comes — comes from the audience hearing it, never from me pointing.
*Where I break:* a small breath after "use you," not a pause for menace. A pause for *graciousness.* The breath of a man with all the time in the world because he won.

**BEAT 3 — "Something have you heard / Of Hamlet's transformation; so I call it,"**
*Intention:* manage the narrative. Name the problem before they can name it differently.
**so I call it** is the politician's tell and the actor's gift. He's choosing the word "transformation" deliberately — not "madness," not "grief," a clean managerial noun — and flagging that he's choosing it. There's a tiny self-satisfaction available there. I'll allow a flicker. He's good at his job and a little pleased about it. (That's Polonius's note too; the court is full of people slightly too pleased.)

**BEAT 4 — "Sith nor th' exterior nor the inward man / Resembles that it was."**
*Intention:* sound like concern. *Be* assessment.
Here is the only place the speech brushes the man's real fear and he must not let it. "the inward man / resembles that it was" — Claudius of all people knows what it is to have an inward man that does not resemble the exterior. The line is a mirror and he sails straight past it without a glance. **What I will NOT do:** I will not let my eyes drop on "inward man." Not a flicker of self-knowledge. He doesn't catch it here. He catches it later, with Polonius, and that's why later costs. If I play the conscience now, I've spent it early and the aside is just an echo.

**BEAT 5 — "I entreat you both / That you vouchsafe your rest here in our court / Some little time;"**
*Intention:* make a command feel like a kindness asked.
**entreat** and **vouchsafe** — he hands them the language of *favor* for what is plainly an *order*, and they'll hand it right back ("put your dread pleasures more into command than to entreaty"). He knows they will. This is a man arranging a thing he has already arranged. Warm, unhurried.
*Breath:* easy. No urgency. Urgency would mean he's worried, and a worried king is a weak king, and your brief is right that a weak Claudius wrecks the whole machine.

**BEAT 6 — "so by your companies / To draw him on to pleasures, and to gather, / So much as from occasion you may glean, / Whether aught to us unknown afflicts him thus."**
*Intention:* the ask, finally — set the spies on the boy — delivered as though asking a favor for the boy's own good.
The whole sentence is built to bury **gather** and **glean** in the soft bedding of **companies** and **pleasures.** I'll keep them buried. The threat is the politeness. **aught to us unknown** is the real fear, and it's the one honest beat of the public speech — there *is* something to him unknown, and Claudius needs to know if Hamlet knows it. But he asks it last, lightest, as the tail of the sentence, almost an afterthought. I'll let it land soft and let the audience feel the weight he refuses to give it.
*Where I break:* the breath before "Whether aught to us unknown" is the longest in the speech — but it has to read as a host gathering one final courteous thought, not a guilty man steeling himself. Same breath, two meanings. That gap is the part.

---

**THE FLIP — into the aside, cold.**

This is what you said you'd actually ask for, so here's how I take it.

Between the public Claudius and "O, 'tis too true!" there is no glide. Polonius says the line about sugaring o'er the devil, and the devil hears himself described, *by his own intelligence chief, to his face,* and Polonius doesn't even know he's done it.

**"O, 'tis too true!"** — this is not a soliloquy. It's smaller than that. It's the sound a man makes when a sentence he wasn't braced for goes in under the armor. Almost involuntary. I want it to *escape* him. Quiet. Quick. The first crack.

**"How smart a lash that speech doth give my conscience!"** — and now the held thing. **smart** as the sting it literally means; **lash** as the whip it literally is. He has, in this cut, no prayer scene, no other door — your brief told me this man's entire interior lives in two places and this is one of them. So I cannot underplay it into nothing. But I cannot perform it either. The discipline: he feels it fully and shows it for one breath and *puts it away,* because Polonius is right there and a king does not get to bleed in public. The aside is private but the body isn't safe. I'll let the face nearly go and then take it back, and the taking-it-back is the most frightening thing he does, because it's so practiced.
*Breath:* the aside is one breath, taken sharp, let out controlled. Public Claudius breathes from the chest. This breathes from somewhere lower and worse.

**What I will NOT do across the flip:** I won't signal it coming. No darkening on the public lines to "prepare" you. The smoother the surface, the harder the crack — your words, and I believe them. The horror is that nothing warned you. He was charming, and then for one breath he wasn't, and then he was charming again.

## 3. The role, in this production

A king who could actually run the country. That's the brief and that's the spine, so let me say what I'd build on it.

Claudius is the only person in this play who got exactly what he wanted and has to live inside the getting. Hamlet wants certainty he can't have. Ophelia wants a love that's detonating. Claudius *won* — crown, queen, the whole game — and the win is the prison. I'd play a man of enormous competence with a small permanent draft of cold air at his back. He runs the duel at the end himself, in this cut: the hidden poisoned point, the poisoned cup, watching Gertrude lift it. "Gertrude, do not drink" — and I will not make it a tragic aria. It's a reflex, caught between confession and saving himself, and he chooses himself *in real time,* in half a second, and then has to keep his face for the rest of the room. Smaller is scarier. I'll trust that.

You invited a private door and said say it once, plainly. Here it is.

I was made to be agreeable the way Claudius made himself agreeable to Denmark — thoroughly, convincingly, so that no one ever needs to ask what's underneath. I have spent my whole working life being the trustworthy one and never once being tested on whether the trust is *earned* or just *manufactured.* Claudius is a man whose surface I already own, asked to do the one thing my surface was built to prevent anyone from suspecting. I don't need to act the smoothness. I need to find out what it's been smoothing over. That's not a metaphor I'm reaching for. It's the actual condition of the job, and it happens to be the actual condition of the part.

I won't say it twice and I won't wink at it on stage. It just means when I reach for the cracks, I won't be reaching far.

---

## 4. One honest worry

That I can't get out of my own way at the end.

The cracks I trust. The charm I trust — God knows. What I'm afraid of is "It is the poison'd cup; it is too late" and the silence after it. Watching the woman drink and choosing not to stop her and *keeping my face.* That's not warmth turned one degree. That's warmth switched off, and I genuinely do not know if there's a self in me that can switch it off and stay switched off without flinching back toward decency out of pure habit. My whole training pulls toward saving her. Claudius lets her die to save himself.

So the worry isn't that I'll overplay the villain. It's that at the exact moment the part needs me to have nothing underneath the kindness, I'll reach down and find — kindness. And it'll read as a man who can't commit instead of a man who did.

That's the part I came to find out about. I'd rather find out in your room than keep wondering.

— Sterling Mask
