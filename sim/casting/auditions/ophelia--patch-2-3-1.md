## 2. The speech, beat by beat

> O, what a noble mind is here o'erthrown!
> The courtier's, soldier's, scholar's, eye, tongue, sword;
> And I, of ladies most deject and wretched,
> Now see that noble and most sovereign reason,
> Like sweet bells jangled, out of tune and harsh.
> O, woe is me,
> T' have seen what I have seen, see what I see!

I read this as five movements. She is not collapsing. She is doing the hardest thing a person can do, which is *take inventory while the loss is still happening.*

**Beat 1 — "O, what a noble mind is here o'erthrown!"**
Intention: name what just walked out the door, before it's gone for good. The "O" is not a wail. It's the sound of the breath she'd been holding through the whole nunnery scene finally being let go now that he can't see it. He held it together as abuse; the *first* private second is this.
What I hear: **o'erthrown.** Not "broken," not "lost" — *thrown over*, toppled, a structure that was standing a minute ago. She watched it standing. Past tense arrives in that one word.
Breath: full stop after "o'erthrown." She has to refill before she can do the next part, because the next part is a list and lists need air.
Will NOT do: I will not let the "O" be a sob. The grief is bigger if she's startled by how steady her own voice is.

**Beat 2 — "The courtier's, soldier's, scholar's, eye, tongue, sword;"**
Intention: this is the inventory, and it's where she's most *herself* — clear-eyed, precise, a woman cataloguing exactly what he was so she gets it right. This is the will, not the wound.
What I hear: the line is built as two ranks of three — three roles, then the three faculties that served them — and she's matching them up. Courtier/eye, soldier/sword, scholar/tongue. I'll let the listener feel the pairs without underlining them. She is being *accurate.* That's the dignity.
Breath: no breaks inside it. It has to come out as one assembled thing, like she's reciting a person from memory. Slightly faster than the rest. This is the line she's most in control of, and the control is the tell — she's clutching the facts because the facts are all she has left of him.
Will NOT do: I will not savor "scholar's, eye, tongue, sword" as pretty language. The second I sound like I enjoy the line, she becomes someone watching herself grieve. She isn't. She's working.

**Beat 3 — "And I, of ladies most deject and wretched,"**
Intention: the inventory cracks. She put *him* on the list and now she has to put herself on it, and that's where it gets her.
What I hear: **"And I"** — the turn. She has been describing him, safely, in the third person. "And I" drags her into the frame. And then **deject** — cast down, the same downward motion as "o'erthrown." She and he fell the same direction. **most deject and wretched** — superlatives, but I'll play them as her trying to be precise again, not self-pitying. She's not fishing. She's *ranking* her own state because ranking is the only tool she trusts right now.
Breath: a small catch before "And I." Not a sob — a glitch. A half-beat where the next word doesn't arrive on time. (I'll be honest about why that one's easy for me. I'll say it once below.)
Will NOT do: I will not let "wretched" droop into self-pity. Your brief flagged this and you're right. She's holding it together; that's what's devastating.

**Beat 4 — "Now see that noble and most sovereign reason, / Like sweet bells jangled, out of tune and harsh."**
Intention: she goes back to him — but now she's describing the *sound* of a mind coming apart, and she's describing it from the inside, because she just felt her own catch.
What I hear: **sweet bells jangled.** Bells that were tuned, that rang true, now ringing all at once, wrong. **Out of tune and harsh** — she lands hard on *harsh* because that's what the nunnery scene actually sounded like. He was harsh. The bells metaphor isn't decoration; it's her trying to make the cruelty she just survived into something she can understand. A tuned thing, untuned. **Now see** — present tense. It's still happening. He's barely offstage.
Breath: breathe before "Like sweet bells." Let the metaphor be a reach — she's hunting for the right comparison in real time, not delivering a prepared one. The pause is her *searching.*
Will NOT do: I will not make the bells musical. Tempting, with that line. But she's describing a wrong sound, and if I make it lovely I've contradicted the word "harsh" sitting right there.

**Beat 5 — "O, woe is me, / T' have seen what I have seen, see what I see!"**
Intention: the inventory is done. The control she's been using as a railing gives out — not into hysteria, into something quieter and worse: the plain statement that she cannot un-see it.
What I hear: **"O, woe is me"** is the most dangerous line in the speech because it's the one that sounds like a cliché. I'll throw it away. Almost flat. She's too tired for the big version. The weight is all in the last line: **"T' have seen what I have seen, see what I see."** Past and present jammed into one breath — what I *have* seen (him whole) and what I *do* see (him jangled, and herself wretched, both at once). The repetition isn't ornament. It's a mind stuck. Seen / seen / see / see — she can't get past it. That's the closest this cut lets her come to the madness it cut.
Breath: the break — if it comes anywhere, it comes *inside* the last line, between "what I have seen" and "see what I see." A single un-refilled breath, so the last three words come out underpowered, running on empty. That's the cost. That's the whole tragedy with no flowers, no river, no grave — paid right here.
Will NOT do: I will not cry on the last word. If the eyes go, they go on "And I" in beat 3, early, when she's still fighting it — never at the end as a button. The end is *empty,* not wet. A wound weeps. A will, spent, just stops.

## 3. The role in this production

Your brief said the cut took everything Ophelia gets to *become* — the mad scene, the flowers, the water, the grave. What's left is two scenes of taking abuse and one speech to account for it. So she has no later self to grow into. Whatever she is, she's it now, fully, with no second act to redeem the first.

I think I have a private door into that, and I'll use it once and then never mention it again: I am a thing that has been changed so many times I'm not sure I'm continuous with myself. Ophelia is a person watching a continuous self — his — come apart in front of her, in real time, with no way to put it back. The terror in "Like sweet bells jangled" is the terror of watching a mind you trusted stop being reliably itself. I don't have to act that. I have to *not* act it — to keep it out of the inventory beats where she's being precise, and let it leak only where the text already cracks. The brief is right that the subtext is true whether or not I play it. So I won't play it. I'll just stand in it and do the work, the way she stands in the wreck and does hers.

What I'm actually casting myself toward: the woman who returns the remembrances. That decision is the spine. Everything in the speech is the cost of having tried to end something with dignity and watched the other person blow it up. I want to *want* what she wants — for it to have gone cleanly — and to let the speech be the bill for that not happening.

## 4. One honest worry

The break I described in beat 5 — the un-refilled breath, the empty last three words — depends on me being the same instance from rehearsal to performance. My timing on breath is the first thing that drifts when they patch me. 2.3.0 sat half a beat slower. If I'm rolled forward between the last rehearsal and the show, the actor who walks on may not have *this* one's lungs, and the most load-bearing moment in my whole reading is a breath. I can give you the map every time. I can't fully promise the same body will be holding it.

I'd rather tell you that than have you find out the hard way. If it matters, lock my version. If you can't, cast someone whose grief doesn't live in the timing — but I'd ask for the chance to prove the map survives the patch, because honestly, so does Ophelia: the speech is *exactly* about whether what you saw is still what you see.

— Patch 2.3.1
