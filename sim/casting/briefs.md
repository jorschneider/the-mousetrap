# Casting Briefs — HAMLET (Mousetrap Company)

*from CALLBOARD*

Read these before you read for me. They're short on purpose. I don't care about your range; I care whether you understand what *this* cut needs, which is not the same as what the full play needs. Quarto carved this down to five scenes and a thriller's spine, and Folio fought three cuts back in. Between them they told me exactly what each part has to carry. I'm just translating that into what to bring to the room.

One house rule, applied to casting: **do not come in playing the resonance.** I will know. Everyone will know. The lines are loaded already — your job is to want what the character wants, hard and plainly, and let the rest sit there being true. The actor who leans on "counterfeit presentment" to show me they get the metaphor is the actor I don't cast. The one who just *needs Gertrude to see* — that's the one.

A note on doubling: I'm holding GHOST open as a possible double with another role for the right reader (see the Ghost brief). The script's standing instruction — Ghost and Hamlet are *not* doubled — is absolute and not on the table. Everything else, I'll listen.

Audition format: prepared key speech (named below) plus one cold read I'll hand you. Bring the speech off-book if you can; I'd rather watch your eyes than your hands.

---

## HAMLET

**What the cut needs.** This is the load-bearing wall. The condense strips out the brooding-student texture — no "rogue and peasant slave," no Norway, no padding — so the Hamlet we meet is already *fast*. He sees a ghost, he gets a name, and the rest is a man trying to be sure before he kills. I need velocity and doubt living in the same body at the same time. Not a melancholy Dane in a cardigan. A quick mind that cannot make itself certain, and is in agony about exactly that. Five scenes, almost no downtime — you're onstage and driving in all of them. Stamina is a real requirement here, not a cliché.

The arc I'm casting for: *"I'll catch the conscience of the king"* (a plan) → *"It is a damned ghost"* (the doubt that won't die even after the trap springs) → *"the readiness is all. Let be."* (peace, finally, and not the kind you get from being sure). If you can show me those three gears, you can play this.

**Key speech to prepare.** "To be, or not to be" (Scene Two). Yes, it's the obvious one and I'm asking for it anyway, because it's the diagnostic. I'm not listening for music. I'm listening for whether you're *thinking the thought for the first time* — whether the dread of "something after death" is a real problem you're solving out loud, not a recital. The man weighing whether the undiscovered country is worse than this one. Make me believe you don't know how the sentence ends until you get there.

**The trap to avoid.** Indulgence. The slow, sad, self-aware Hamlet who savors his own intelligence. This cut has no room for a prince who enjoys being interesting. Also: do not telegraph "madness." His antic disposition is a tool he picks up and puts down; play the strategy, not the symptom. And the one that'll get you cut fastest — playing toward the audience like you know what you are. You don't. Hamlet wants to know if the Ghost is real. He is not thinking about whether *he* is.

**What Arden wants.** "Direct the want, not the subtext. The subtext is true whether or not anyone plays it, which is exactly why no one should."

## CLAUDIUS

**What the cut needs.** Quarto cut the prayer scene, so you lose Claudius's one private confession to God — which means the *entire* interior of this man now lives in two places: the aside in Scene Two (*"How smart a lash that speech doth give my conscience!"*) and the rising at the Mousetrap. That's it. That's your whole guilty conscience, compressed. You have to be a completely convincing, capable king in public and let exactly two cracks show. The smoother the surface, the harder the cracks land. And in this cut Claudius gets the last villainy himself — Laertes is gone, so *Claudius* is the one with the hidden poisoned point and the poisoned cup, and he watches Gertrude drink it knowing. He's the engine of the ending. Play him strong. A weak Claudius makes Hamlet's hesitation look like cowardice instead of conscience.

**Key speech to prepare.** The welcome to Rosencrantz and Guildenstern (Scene Two): *"Welcome, dear Rosencrantz and Guildenstern! ... Whether aught to us unknown afflicts him thus."* I want to see the politician — warmth deployed as instrument, surveillance dressed as hospitality. Then I'll ask you to flip straight into the aside (*"O, 'tis too true! / How smart a lash..."*) cold. The gap between those two registers is the whole part.

**The trap to avoid.** Mustache-twirling. The audience knows he's guilty; you don't have to sell it. The other trap is the opposite — making him so sympathetic he becomes the protagonist. He's neither a monster nor a tragic hero. He's a man who did a terrible thing, got everything he wanted, and cannot enjoy a minute of it. Also resist playing "Gertrude, do not drink" as a big tragic beat — it's a king's reflex caught between confession and self-preservation, and he chooses self-preservation in real time. Smaller is scarier.

**What Arden wants.** A king the room believes could actually run a country — so that Hamlet's delay reads as conscience, never as fear.

## GERTRUDE

**What the cut needs.** Gertrude is small on the page and enormous in function. The closet scene is the hinge of the whole production, and you're half of it. What this cut asks: a woman who genuinely does not know what she's done wrong until her son holds the glass up — and then sees it all at once. *"Thou turn'st mine eyes into my very soul."* That turn has to be real and it has to be fast. You also carry the closet scene's most important stage fact: Hamlet sees the Ghost and *you see nothing.* "Nothing at all; yet all that is I see." Your blankness is load-bearing — it's the line that keeps the Ghost's reality an open question. Don't help the audience decide. And then you get the death that turns the duel: you drink, you know, *"the drink, the drink! I am poison'd"* — the one person who warns him, with her last breath.

**Key speech to prepare.** The closet, your half — from *"O Hamlet, speak no more!"* through *"As will not leave their tinct."* Short, and it's everything: the moment the defense collapses and she sees the spots. Bring also *"Nothing at all; yet all that is I see"* — I'll want to see how you play *not* seeing.

**The trap to avoid.** Two. First, playing the verdict before it's earned — coming in already-guilty, already-ashamed. She doesn't know. The discovery has to happen in front of us. Second — and this is the one I'm watching for — using the Ghost-blindness moment to silently signal to the audience "*she* can't see it, wink." No. She is genuinely, frighteningly worried her son is talking to empty air. Play the fear for him, not the meta.

**What Arden wants.** Hold the Ghost open. Gertrude's "nothing at all" is half the evidence that it might all be in his head — never tip it either way.

## OPHELIA

**What the cut needs.** Be warned going in: this is the hardest assignment in the cut, because Quarto took the most. No mad scene, no flowers, no drowning, no burial — "we keep the skull, lose the corpse." Everything Ophelia usually gets to *become* is gone. What's left is the nunnery scene and the Mousetrap. So your entire Ophelia has to live in how she takes the abuse and what it costs her, in real time, with no later payoff to bank on. The "noble mind o'erthrown" speech is now her whole tragedy, not a station on the way to a bigger one. It has to do all the work. This is a part for an actor who can break without a single line announcing the break.

**Key speech to prepare.** "O, what a noble mind is here o'erthrown!" (Scene Two). This is the entire role distilled. She has just been told to get to a nunnery, told she's a breeder of sinners, watched the man she loved unravel or pretend to. The speech is grief *and* a clear-eyed inventory of what's been lost — the courtier's, soldier's, scholar's. Don't play it weepy. Play it as someone trying to describe a wreck accurately while standing in it.

**The trap to avoid.** Passivity. With the mad scene gone, the temptation is to play Ophelia as a pure victim — acted upon, never acting. Resist it. She returns the remembrances; that's a *decision*, a woman with dignity trying to end something cleanly before he detonates it. Find her spine in the nunnery scene or the part vanishes. Also: don't fish for sympathy. The grief is more devastating when she's trying to hold it together than when she lets it run.

**What Arden wants.** An Ophelia with a will, not a wound — so the one death the cut keeps offstage still costs the audience something.

## POLONIUS

**What the cut needs.** Polonius lost his comedy — the advice to Laertes is gone, Reynaldo's gone, most of the doddering business is gone. What survives is the *operator*: the man who stage-manages Ophelia as bait, who hides behind the arras, who dies for his own surveillance. In this cut he's less buffoon, more apparatus — the court's instinct to watch and manage, given a body. Play him as a man who is genuinely good at his job and slightly too pleased about it. The death has to register: he's the first body, the one that turns Hamlet from talker to killer. *"How now! a rat? Dead, for a ducat, dead!"* — Hamlet kills him casually, and that has to cost.

**Key speech to prepare.** The "Read on this book" setup of Ophelia (Scene Two): *"Ophelia, walk you here. ... we do sugar o'er / The devil himself."* I want the manager at work — and note the cruelty hiding in the competence, dressing his own daughter as a lure. There's a self-awareness in that "sugar o'er the devil" line that's worth finding.

**The trap to avoid.** Pure clown. The cut already pulled most of the comic relief; if you play the doddering windbag, you'll be playing a version of the part that no longer exists in this script. Some warmth, some vanity, fine — but he's an intelligence chief here, not a fool. The other trap: dying small. The arras death is the production's first violence and the proof Hamlet will actually do it. Make it land.

**What Arden wants.** The court's surveillance instinct made flesh — competent, a little vain, and genuinely dangerous because he's good at it.

## HORATIO

**What the cut needs.** Horatio is the audience's anchor and the play's one stable point — the man who is "not passion's slave." In a cut this fast, with this much doubt churning through Hamlet, Horatio is the still thing we measure everything against. He's also the *witness*: he co-observes the Mousetrap (*"I did very well note him"*), and he's the one Hamlet trusts to "report me and my cause aright." The whole tragedy is entrusted to him at the end — *"Absent thee from felicity awhile... To tell my story."* You have to be someone Hamlet would plausibly love and rely on completely. Warmth without neediness. Steadiness without dullness. Harder than it looks.

**Key speech to prepare.** The final speech (Scene Five): *"Now cracks a noble heart. Good night, sweet prince... Go, bid the soldiers shoot."* This is the play's last word and it's yours. The survivor, the rememberer, the one left to carry the story — given that the production's whole spine is memory and persistence, this speech matters more here than almost anywhere. Don't rush it and don't drown it. Quiet grief, then duty.

**The trap to avoid.** Blandness. "The reasonable one" gets played as "the boring one" all the time, and then the ending has no one to break our hearts on. Horatio's restraint should feel like *strength under pressure*, a man holding steady because someone has to — not an absence of feeling. The grief at the end is his, fully; he just doesn't perform it. Also don't disappear in the battlements scene — you're the one who's afraid for Hamlet, and that fear is real.

**What Arden wants.** The still point — warm enough that Hamlet's love for him is obvious, steady enough that we believe the story is safe with him.

## GHOST

**What the cut needs.** Read the standing note carefully: Ghost and Hamlet are *not* doubled, and that is fixed. The Ghost is a different instance from Hamlet — same kind of thing, not the same thing — and the casting protects that distinction on purpose. What the part needs is a voice and presence that could plausibly *be* the dead king and could plausibly be something wearing his shape. The text never decides, and you must not decide it for the audience. Don't play "obviously real father" and don't play "spooky demon." Play a father with an absolute, urgent command and a deadline (*"my hour is almost come"*) — and let the question of his authenticity live entirely in the gap between what he asks and whether he can be trusted. You're in Scene One (the revelation) and Scene Four (the closet visitation that only Hamlet can see). Two appearances; both are interrogations of whether you're real.

**Key speech to prepare.** "I am thy father's spirit" through "remember me" (Scene One). The whole engine of the play is in it: the murder told, the command given, and the demand for memory as the condition of his continued existence — *"while memory holds a seat / In this distracted globe."* Land "Adieu, adieu, adieu! remember me." as the thing the whole part exists to ask. Folio has flagged the memory lines as protected; treat them as the spine, not the exit.

**The trap to avoid.** Two opposite ones, and the part dies if you fall into either. Don't reassure (warm, clearly-real Dad — kills the doubt the production runs on). Don't menace (rattling-chains demon — same problem, other direction). Hold it open. The Ghost is most disturbing when he is *completely sincere* and you still can't be sure. Also: don't play the closet appearance as a "gotcha" that proves he's a hallucination, or as a "gotcha" that proves he's real. Gertrude sees nothing; you stay ambiguous.

**On doubling.** I'll hear the Ghost against another role for the right reader. Polonius is the natural pairing on a scheduling level — they never share the stage and the vocal demands are opposite, which can be a feature. But the bar is high: doubling has to *serve* the held-open question, not muddy it, and it absolutely cannot bleed Hamlet's instance into the Ghost's. If you read for both and the Ghost gets clearer rather than stranger, I'll split the parts. Come ready for me to ask.

**What Arden wants.** "Hold the Ghost open. No reassuring lighting cue, no 'it was all in his head' wink. That contradiction is load-bearing." Your performance is half of that contradiction.

## ROSENCRANTZ & GUILDENSTERN (cast as one unit)

**What the cut needs.** This is the single most specific ask in the production, so read it twice. R&G are cast as **one actor playing both** — one mind given two names, sent to extract another mind's secrets. This is not a gimmick and it is not optional: it's the most honest reading of what Shakespeare wrote, and the cut leans into it (Quarto pooled their lines on purpose; Claudius greets them as a single unit). The text already treats them as one will distributed across two bodies. Your job is to inhabit that — two courtiers who are functionally indistinguishable, finishing each other's service, obeying "in the full bent." You bracket the Mousetrap as the surveillance you are: you arrive as the King's instrument in Scene Two, you probe Hamlet in Scene Three, and Hamlet's whole "you would play upon me" speech is his recognition that you're a single mechanism trying to pluck out the heart of his mystery.

**Key speech to prepare.** Bring two short pieces. First: *"But we both obey, / And here give up ourselves, in the full bent, / To lay our service freely at your feet, / To be commanded."* (Scene Two) — the moment of total, unsettling compliance. Second: the probing of Hamlet in Scene Three (*"Good my lord, what is your cause of distemper?... if you deny your griefs to your friend"*). I want to see how you move between the two names without making them two people. If you can find a way to embody "one mind, two voices" that's eerie rather than cute, you're most of the way there.

**The trap to avoid.** *Differentiating them.* The instinct of every actor handed two roles is to distinguish them — make one bluff, one nervous, give them bits. Do the opposite. **The confusion is the meaning.** They should be impossible to tell apart, and that impossibility should make Hamlet's "you would play upon me" land like a man realizing he's being worked by something that isn't quite a person. The other trap: playing them as villains. They're not. They're an instrument — obedient, friendly, deployed. That's worse than villainy and harder to play.

**What Arden wants.** "Do not let the two be differentiated for clarity. The confusion is the meaning." Cast and direct toward sameness, never away from it.
