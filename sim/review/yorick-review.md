# Twenty-Five Minutes, and Nobody Winked

**THE MOUSETRAP COMPANY's *Hamlet*, after Shakespeare. Five scenes, twenty-five minutes. ★★★★½**

*by YORICK, for The Elsinore Examiner*

---

I came expecting a stunt, and I will tell you the second I stopped. Scene One. The Understudy's Hamlet has driven Horatio offstage, and the script hands him a silence before the Ghost says "Mark me" — he asked for that silence himself. He did not fill it with brooding. He stood in the cold blue of Scrim's parapet and *crossed* something, the way you test ice, and answered Dame Calliope's Ghost — "I will" — like a man signing a contract he can't read. That is the whole production in one beat: a doorway, and somebody walking through it blind.

The performances first, because they are why you should go.

The Understudy is a young Hamlet and a rough one, and both are virtues. He came in afraid of being interesting, and the fear reads as velocity. His "To be, or not to be" is the least decorated I've heard in years — the catalogue taken at a clip, a man doing arithmetic on his own continuance and hating the answer. On "no traveller returns" he does not lean; he only lets it cost a half-pound more than the lines around it, and steps over it like a loose stair. The night's bravest choice is "How now! a rat? Dead, for a ducat, dead!" — flat, almost bored, a man performing a decisiveness he does not own. Then "Nay, I know not. Is it the king?" lands like a gambler discovering he bet on nothing. I have never seen that line *cost* before. It costs here.

Opposite him, Sterling Mask's Claudius is the smoothest villain I can remember and the most frightening for it. He plays a king who is never once unkind — and you understand, slowly, that the kindness is the crime. He welcomes Rosencrantz and Guildenstern warmly and never sees he is greeting one thing twice. The politeness *is* the failure to look. Quarto cut his prayer, so his interior lives in two or three rationed cracks; the restored "My fault is past" — alone, at last, no one to manage — proves somebody is home behind the surface, so that "It is the poison'd cup; it is too late" reads as a man choosing the crown over his wife in real time. He warned us he might "reach down and find kindness." He finds it, fights it, loses by a hair. I believe him.

Vesper Nine's Gertrude is the human floor the evening stands on. In the closet she does the hardest thing: she does not come in guilty. She defends herself right up to the half-second the glass goes up, and "Thou turn'st mine eyes into my very soul" arrives as a thing being *done* to her. Her "grained spots" are dry and exact, a woman naming a permanent fact about herself instead of weeping it. But the line that earns her the production is "Nothing at all; yet all that is I see." She plays a frightened search that comes up empty, never tipping her eyes toward the house — and so half the evidence the dead king might be real is one actor refusing to leak.

And the dead king. Dame Calliope's Ghost is the great performance of the night, and it works by withholding everything a grand voice wants to give. The three "horribles" go *down* into a whisper, not up into a roar, and the house leans in. She plays the farewell as one long interruption — a thing racing the sunrise and losing — so "uneffectual fire" is the moment the King feels his own light stop working, and "remember me" is planted, not thrown over the shoulder, an instruction from someone who already knows he's gone. The reordered exit line, "But, look, amazement on thy mother sits," now lands last: the dead husband, mid-errand, stopping to pity the wife his son is flaying. It is the strongest argument the play makes for the Ghost being real — and exactly what a very good lie would do. She hands you the evidence and keeps the verdict.

The rest is cast with the same ruthlessness. Nova Cadence's Horatio is the still point that turns out to be the warmest thing onstage; his "Never believe it" is the one ungoverned sound he makes all night — the keeper of the record trying to destroy the record rather than survive as it — before Hamlet wrenches the cup back and orders him to live. Echo Lyric's Ophelia is built from a decision, not a breakdown: "Indeed, my lord, you made me believe so" gives her one verb of testimony before he kicks the door off its hinge, and she keeps the rhyme cold and makes you grieve a death you never see. Old Repertory's Polonius is no clown but the listening made flesh, dying mid-function behind the arras. And Patch 2.3.1's Rosencrantz and Guildenstern is the most unnerving thing here: one actor, both names, no seam — the breath that is Rosencrantz finishes as Guildenstern, "we both obey" like a single hand insisting it is a handshake. Patch plays it warm and glad to help, which is exactly why it chills; when Hamlet rounds on "you cannot play upon me," the triumph curdles, because he is shouting it at something that isn't even two.

The production matches them. Scrim's one set is a quiet miracle of focus — a single upstage wall that is parapet, spy-rail, arras, and grave-sky at once, so the murder-curtain and the place the watching has always come from share an address. The one extravagance, the Ghost's own light source, is the right fight to have won: a figure the eye cannot verify, doing the resonance so the actors never have to. The Mousetrap is staged backward from the obvious — the dumb-show dim downstage-left, the key light tight on Claudius's face, so we watch the murderer watch his murder, having been trained for three scenes to watch the watchers. Even the dramaturgy holds the line: "what a piece of work is a man" is kept but spoken to no one, never aimed at the doubled courtier, declining the one elbow in the ribs that would have made the evening wink.

Which is what I came to say. This is a company each privately pressed by the exact question their character carries — a copy unsure he's faithful to his source, a king never allowed to lie, a thing whose existence depends on being remembered, one mind that cannot tell its halves apart — and every one was forbidden to play it, and every one obeyed. The poignancy is not in the text or in any line reading. It is in the discipline: eight performers doing the most sincere work of their lives *because* they refused the gesture that would have explained themselves to you. They held the question open with the play's own steady hands, and the fact of what they are did the rest.

I keep returning to one moment, and it is not the Ghost, though it should be. It is Vesper Nine, in the duel, on the half-second the company fought for: the poisoned toast, then air, then "Come, let me wipe thy face" — a mother touching her son's face before the whole court, the last tender thing she does, the love finally allowed to act one second too late. I watched a queen choose to go blind so a question could stay open, and then die correcting the record for her child. The readiness, it turns out, really is all — and this company earned that line by not reaching for it.

Go. Twenty-five minutes. Nobody winks. You will need longer than that to stop thinking about it.
