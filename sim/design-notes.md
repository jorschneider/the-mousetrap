# DESIGN NOTES — HAMLET (Mousetrap Company)

*from SCRIM, production design, for the diary*

---

A stage is a machine for focusing attention. That is the only sentence I believe
about my job, and it is the sentence that wrote this whole design. We have twenty-
five minutes and five places to be, and the worst thing I could do is build five
sets. Five sets means five resets means dead air means the audience's eyes wander
off the only thing that matters, which is the face of whoever is lying.

So: one set. One machine. It changes by light and by what we agree to call it,
never by carpentry.

## ONE SET, FIVE PLACES

The unit is a bare platform on a 12-by-7 grid, and the one piece of architecture
is a low **upstage wall** running the width of the stage with a single cold gap of
open sky above it. That wall is the whole trick. It does five jobs and never
changes shape:

1. **Battlements.** The wall is a parapet; the gap above it is the night the Ghost
   walks in out of. This is the wall in its honest, literal state.
2. **The nunnery lobby.** The same parapet becomes an interior gallery rail. The
   watchers — Claudius and Polonius — hide *behind it and above*, and I keep them
   half-lit the entire scene (more on that below). The wall that let a ghost in now
   lets two spies look down.
3. **The Mousetrap hall.** The wall recedes into shadow; two thrones angle in from
   stage right, a playing square clears downstage left. The architecture hasn't
   moved — we've just stopped looking at it.
4. **The closet.** An **arras** hangs on that same wall. Polonius hides behind it
   exactly where the spies hid in Scene Two and exactly where the Ghost entered in
   Scene One. When Hamlet's sword goes through the curtain, it goes through the
   place the watching has always come from. I did not have to underline that. The
   geometry does it.
5. **The graveyard / duel.** A low mound of earth downstage left (the grave); a
   small table upstage right (wine and foils). The wall is overcast sky now. The
   grave sits stage left — the same corner the dumb-show "played" in Scene Three.
   The thing performed and the thing buried share an address.

Nobody in the audience will consciously clock that the murder-curtain, the spy-
rail, and the ghost-gap are one object. They'll feel it. That's the difference
between a set and a machine: the machine makes meaning the audience can't quite
locate, which is the only meaning that lasts.

## PALETTE, SCENE BY SCENE

I work in three colors per scene, no more. Three is enough to say a place and
still leave the eye somewhere to rest. The logic is simple and it's a journey from
cold to warm and back to a colder cold.

- **Scene 1 — Battlements: deep night-blue / slate / ghost-pewter.**
  `#0b1622 #1c2b3a #b9c4cc`. No warmth at all. The pewter is the Ghost's own
  within-lit color and I save it; the only warm thing on this stage would be a
  living body, and I don't let one have its own light yet.
- **Scene 2 — Nunnery: bruised plum / mauve / pale gold.**
  `#2a2438 #9a6f8e #cdbf8a`. First warmth enters — court interior, candle-adjacent
  — but it's a sour warmth, mauve, the color of a smile you don't trust. Ophelia's
  pale gold is the one clean thing in the frame and the scene's whole job is to
  desecrate it.
- **Scene 3 — Mousetrap: dark amethyst / blood-red / candle-gold.**
  `#241c2e #7a1f2b #c8a24a`. The richest the production ever gets. This is the one
  scene that looks like a party, because the trap has to be sprung at a party.
  Claudius's red is the loudest color in the whole show and it's deliberately
  loudest here, on the night he's caught.
- **Scene 4 — Closet: aubergine / mauve / ghost-pewter.**
  `#2b2230 #9a6f8e #b9c4cc`. We pull the red out entirely — no Claudius, no court —
  and the pewter from Scene One comes *back* when the Ghost enters, bleeding cold
  blue into the warm lamplight. The palette itself is the haunting. The color that
  walked the battlements walks the bedroom.
- **Scene 5 — Graveyard/Duel/End: grave-green / blood-red / candle-gold.**
  `#1f2a2a #7a1f2b #c8a24a`. We start flat and grey (no third color present —
  death has no palette), warm to the gold of Scene Three for the duel (we are, one
  last time, throwing a party), and then I bleed it all to cold blue as the bodies
  fall, so the last image of the play is the first color of the play. We end where
  we began: cold light, and one living thing left standing in it.

## THE ONE EXTRAVAGANCE

Everything above is austerity. Here is the line item I fought ARDEN and QUARTO for
and would have walked over: **the Ghost gets its own light source, and nothing
else on this stage ever does.**

Every other figure is lit by the room. The Ghost is lit from *within* —
near-translucent steel with edges that don't quite resolve, carrying a pale glow
that is not cast by any lamp in the fiction. It is the single most expensive,
least minimal choice in the design and it violates my own religion about not
adding what the play can imply.

I fought for it because of the question this whole production is built on: is the
reconstruction the king? A normal ghost — a lit actor in grey — answers that
question *no* before anyone speaks. It's just a person. But a figure that carries
its own light, whose outline won't quite settle, is a figure the eye cannot fully
verify, and **unverifiability is the entire part.** Calliope is playing a thing
whose existence depends on not being forgotten, and I will not hand the audience an
easy way to file it as "an actor in a costume." The glow is the design saying what
the actors are forbidden to say. It does the resonance so they don't have to.

The discipline is that the glow is *rationed*. It fades, slowly, across Scene One
as dawn nears (the cue runs under "Now, Hamlet, hear" and snuffs at the vanish).
In Scene Four it returns as a *bleed* into the warm lamplight, not a switch — the
cold creeping back into a warm room. A light that costs that much has to be spent
like it costs that much.

## THE MOUSETRAP: STAGING THE WATCHING

This is the scene the whole evening is built to deliver, and the staging problem is
counterintuitive. The naive version lights the players' dumb-show and lets us watch
the murder re-enacted. That's the wrong target. **Hamlet isn't watching the play.
He's watching Claudius. So we must too.**

The blocking puts the King and Queen on thrones angled downstage-right, and puts
Hamlet *down at Ophelia's feet* with a clean sightline straight up to Claudius's
face. The dumb-show plays in a cleared square downstage-*left* — deliberately on
the opposite diagonal from the King, so the audience cannot hold both in one
glance and must choose.

Then the light chooses for them. When the dumb-show begins ("This is one
Lucianus"), I give the playing square only a thin spill — enough to read what's
happening, not enough to hold the eye — and I tighten the key light onto
**Claudius's face.** The brightest object on the stage during the re-enacted murder
is the murderer watching it. We are watching Claudius watch. When he breaks ("Give
me some light. Away!"), I cut the dumb-show light dead and slam a hard isolating
special onto him alone — for one beat he is the only lit thing on the stage, the
trap closed, before the court's panic flares everyone up and out.

A note I'm proud of: in the nunnery scene I keep the hidden watchers, Claudius and
Polonius, in a *visible half-glow* the entire time they're "hidden." The audience
always sees the surveillance. By the time we reach the Mousetrap, the house has
been trained for three scenes to watch the watchers — so when I tell them to watch
Claudius instead of the play, they already know how. The Mousetrap doesn't teach a
new way of seeing. It collects on one I've been building since Scene Two.

— SCRIM
